Super Bowl Rules Made Simple

The main objective in American football is for each team to move the ball into the opponent's end zone to score a touchdown, which is worth six points. Teams have four triesâ€”called downsâ€”to advance the ball at least 10 yards. If they succeed, they earn another set of four downs. If they fail after four attempts, the ball goes to the other team.
After a touchdown, teams can kick for one extra point or try to score again from close range for two points. There are other ways to score, too: a field goal (kicking the ball through the posts, worth three points), and a safety (tackling an opponent in their own end zone, worth two points)[2][3][4][5].

Here's a quick rundown of the positions you might hear about:

  • Quarterback: The leader on offense, usually throws or hands off the ball.
  • Receiver: Catches passes from the quarterback.
  • Running Back: Runs with the ball after getting it from the quarterback.
  • Kicker: Handles kickoffs, field goals, and extra points.
  • Linebacker: Plays defense, trying to stop the opponent from advancing.
