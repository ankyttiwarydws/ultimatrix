J&K internal debt up 262% in 5 years; bulk of borrowings used for repayments: CAG

Srinagar, Apr 12: The financial health of Jammu and Kashmir has come under sharp focus, with the latest report of the Comptroller and Auditor General of India (CAG) flagging a steep rise in debt and a worrying pattern in the utilisation of borrowed funds. According to the CAG report, “the internal debt of the UT Government had been on the rise as it increased by Rs 27,666.20 crore (261.94 per cent) from Rs 10,562.21 crore in 2020-21 to Rs 38,228.41 crore in 2024-25.” The surge reflects a rapidly expanding debt burden over a five-year period. The audit further points to a limited fiscal space for development spending. “During 2024-25, the repayment of borrowings constituted over 80 per cent of the total borrowing receipts, leaving only Rs 7,943 crore (20 per cent) of the total borrowings available for meeting expenditure and development activities,” it notes. While borrowings declined in the last financial year, the liabilities continued to grow at a faster pace than the economy. The report states, “although there was a significant reduction of about 25 per cent in the borrowings raised by the UT Government during 2024-25 as compared to the previous year, the rate of growth of outstanding liabilities remained high at 22.93 per cent, which was substantially higher than the growth rate of GSDP.” Calling attention to fiscal sustainability, the CAG underlines that “the trend indicates a rising debt burden and underscores the need for the Government to strengthen fiscal discipline and adopt prudent debt management measures to contain the growth of outstanding liabilities.” The report also raises concerns over how borrowed funds are being utilised. It reiterates that such funds should ideally support asset creation and development. “Borrowed funds should ideally be used to fund Capital creation and developmental activities. Using borrowed funds for meeting current consumption and repayment of interest on outstanding loans is not a healthy trend,” the audit observes. Data presented in the report shows that between 2020-21 and 2024-25, a dominant share of borrowings was used to repay past debt. " This shows that the repayment of borrowings (principal) to current borrowings of the UT ranged between 80 per cent to 88 per cent... Thus, the major portion of borrowed funds were used for repayment of borrowings instead of Capital creation/ development activities.” Capital expenditure from borrowed funds remained relatively modest, ranging between 12 and 20 per cent during the period. However, there was a marginal improvement in the last fiscal. “The UT Government increased its spending to 20 per cent of the borrowed funds on Capital expenditure during 2024-25 as against 16 per cent spent during 2023-24, reflecting a positive shift towards productive utilisation of borrowings.” Responding to the audit findings, the government stated in January 2026 that the issue needs to be assessed differently. It maintained that “the issue of utilisation of borrowed funds needs to be worked out on the basis of debt raised only and Ways and means advances may be excluded, as it is used to meet short term debt to meet cash requirements of the Government.” The findings collectively highlight mounting fiscal pressures and the increasing challenge of balancing debt servicing with development needs in the Union Territory.
