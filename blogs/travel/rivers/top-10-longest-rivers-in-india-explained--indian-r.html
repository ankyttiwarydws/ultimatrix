1. Ganga (Ganges) â€“ 2,525 km

The Ganga is India's longest and most revered river, stretching for 2,525 kilometers across the northern plains. Originating from the Gangotri Glacier in Uttarakhand, it meanders through states like Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Jharkhand, and West Bengal before merging into the Bay of Bengal. The Ganga is worshipped as a goddess and holds immense spiritual significance, with cities like Varanasi, Haridwar, and Patna situated along its banks. Its fertile plains support millions of livelihoods, making it the heart of northern India.
States covered: Uttarakhand, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Jharkhand, West Bengal

2. Godavari â€“ 1,465 km

The Godavari, often called the "Dakshina Ganga" or the Ganges of the South, is the second-longest river wholly within India. Spanning 1,465 kilometers, it originates in the Western Ghats at Trimbak in Maharashtra and flows eastward across Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, and Chhattisgarh before emptying into the Bay of Bengal. The river basin supports rich agriculture and is home to many pilgrimage sites.
States covered: Maharashtra, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Odisha

3. Krishna â€“ 1,400 km

The Krishna River is central India's lifeline, stretching 1,400 kilometers from its source in Mahabaleshwar (Maharashtra) through Karnataka, Telangana, and Andhra Pradesh. It forms one of the largest river basins, nourishing cities and agricultural zones and hosting famous pilgrimage towns like Vijayawada.
States covered: Maharashtra, Karnataka, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh

4. Yamuna â€“ 1,376 km

The Yamuna is the longest tributary of the Ganga, running parallel for much of its 1,376 km journey. Originating at the Yamunotri Glacier in Uttarakhand, it passes through important cities including Delhi, Agra, and Mathura. The confluence of the Yamuna and Ganga at Allahabad (Prayagraj) is a sacred spot for Hindus.
States covered: Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, Haryana, Delhi, Uttar Pradesh

5. Narmada â€“ 1,312 km

Flowing westward, unlike most Indian rivers, the Narmada carves a scenic path of 1,312 km from the Amarkantak Plateau in Madhya Pradesh to the Arabian Sea in Gujarat. It is one of the holiest rivers and forms the traditional boundary between North and South India.
States covered: Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Gujarat

The Indus River is historically significant, having supported the ancient Indus Valley Civilization. While its total length is about 3,180 km, only 1,114 km flows through the Indian territory, mainly in the region of Ladakh and Punjab, before entering Pakistan.
States/UTs covered: Ladakh, Himachal Pradesh, Punjab

The Brahmaputra traverses Tibet, India, and Bangladesh, with 916 km of its course in India, primarily through Arunachal Pradesh and Assam. Known for its mighty floods and fertile valleys, it is the lifeblood of the northeast and merges with the Ganga in Bangladesh.
States covered: Arunachal Pradesh, Assam

8. Mahanadi â€“ 890 km

The Mahanadi, meaning "Great River," originates in the Sihawa hills of Chhattisgarh and flows 890 km eastwards through Odisha to the Bay of Bengal. It is crucial for irrigation and hydroelectric projects in eastern India.
States covered: Chhattisgarh, Odisha

The Kaveri is a sacred river in southern India, stretching 800 km from the Brahmagiri hills in Karnataka through Tamil Nadu before reaching the Bay of Bengal. Known for ancient temples and the fertile delta that supports rice cultivation, it is often called the "Ganga of the South."
States covered: Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala (briefly)

The Tapti flows westwards for 724 km from the Satpura Range in Madhya Pradesh, through Maharashtra and into Gujarat, before draining into the Arabian Sea. Like the Narmada, it is one of the few major rivers flowing west in India.
