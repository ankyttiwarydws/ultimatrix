Open Interest (OI) — Counting How Many Bets Are Still Active

Open Interest sounds complicated. It isn't.

Here's the simplest way to think about it:

OI is just the number of option contracts that haven't been closed yet.

Think of it like a cricket stadium. Every match, tickets get sold. Some people leave early (they closed their position). Others stay till the end (they're still in the trade). Open Interest is the count of people still sitting in the stadium.

Every options contract involves two sides — one buyer and one seller. When Ramesh buys a Nifty 24,000 Call option and Sunil sells it to him, one contract is created. As long as neither of them exits, that one contract stays open. That's one unit of Open Interest.

When either side closes their trade, OI goes down.

What OI tells you: How much activity and interest exists at a particular strike price. A strike with very high OI has a lot of people with active bets. A strike with low OI is relatively quiet.

Why Everyone Talks About Sellers

Here's something that confuses a lot of beginners: OI doesn't actually tell you who bought and who sold. It just counts active contracts. So why do traders always talk about "big sellers" when reading OI?

Because in real markets, the large institutional players — banks, prop desks, big hedge funds — tend to prefer selling options rather than buying them. There are mathematical and statistical reasons for this (option sellers collect premium and benefit from time decay), but the practical point is:

When OI is very high at a specific strike, many traders assume large institutions are selling options there. And since these players have deep pockets and are hard to move, those strike levels often act as strong support or resistance.

This is not a guarantee. It's a tendency. And experienced traders use it as context, not as a rule.

How to Read Call OI and Put OI (The Simple Version)

Open Interest is split into two types: Call OI and Put OI. Here's what each typically suggests:

Rising Call OI at a Strike Above Current Price

Imagine the market is at 24,000 and you see very high Call OI building at 24,500.

This often means a lot of traders are selling the 24,500 Call — essentially betting the market won't reach 24,500 by expiry. These sellers are comfortable taking on that risk, which suggests they don't expect a strong rally beyond that level.

Practical implication: High Call OI above current price can act like a ceiling. It doesn't guarantee the market stops there — but it's a level worth watching.

Rising Put OI at a Strike Below Current Price

The mirror image. If you see heavy Put OI building at 23,500 and the market is at 24,000, it suggests many traders are selling puts at 23,500 — betting the market stays above that level.

Practical implication: High Put OI below current price can act like a floor.

One important caution: OI is not a prediction. Markets can and do break through high-OI levels, especially during strong trending moves or unexpected news. Think of these levels as areas of interest, not walls.

PCR (Put Call Ratio) — Taking the Market's Temperature

If OI tells you how much activity is happening, PCR tells you which way traders are leaning.

The formula is simple:

PCR = Total Put OI ÷ Total Call OI

If Put OI is 80 lakh contracts and Call OI is 70 lakh contracts: PCR = 80/70 = approximately 1.14

Here's a rough guide for reading PCR:

| PCR Level | What It Usually Suggests | |---|---| | Around 1.0 | Roughly balanced — no strong lean either way | | Above 1.0 | More Put selling — generally a bullish sign | | Above 1.3–1.5 | Extremely bullish sentiment — start watching for reversals | | Below 0.7 | More Call selling — generally a bearish sign | | Below 0.5 | Extreme bearishness — possible bounce territory |

The Counterintuitive Part Most Beginners Miss

You'd think a very high PCR (lots of put activity) means the market is scared and bearish. Logically that sounds right.

But here's the twist: when PCR is very high, it often means the market is actually too comfortable. Everyone is selling puts. Everyone expects the market to stay up. There are no more bears left to convert.

Think of a marriage where every single guest has already voted "yes" to the couple staying together. Who's left to change their mind? Nobody. The consensus is so strong that any small surprise can catch people off guard.

Markets work similarly. When PCR gets to extreme levels — too high or too low — it often signals a potential reversal, not continuation. This is called a contrarian reading of PCR.

It's not a mechanical rule. It's a warning sign to watch for.

Implied Volatility (IV) — The Market's Expectation of Chaos

Implied Volatility is probably the concept that trips up the most beginners. The name makes it sound like a complex calculation (it is, mathematically — but you don't need to understand the math).

Here's the only definition you need:

IV is the market's expectation of how much a stock or index will move in the future.

That's it.

Notice what it doesn't say: IV doesn't tell you which direction the move will be. Just that a big move is expected (high IV) or that things are expected to stay calm (low IV).

The Umbrella Analogy

Imagine you run an umbrella shop.

In the middle of May — peak summer — nobody wants umbrellas. You'd have to practically give them away. That's low IV: the market is calm, nobody is paying much for protection.

Now imagine three days before a cyclone warning is issued. Suddenly everyone wants umbrellas. You can charge double the price. That's high IV: there's fear in the market, people are willing to pay more for options.

The critical thing to understand: high IV makes options more expensive to buy. You're paying a premium for protection (or speculation) precisely when the market is most chaotic.

This is why experienced traders often say: "Don't buy options when IV is spiking." You might be right about the direction but still lose money because you overpaid for the option and IV drops after the event (this is called IV crush).

Rough IV Reference for Nifty Options

These ranges are approximate and change with market conditions — don't treat them as fixed:

| IV Level | What It Roughly Means | |---|---| | Below 10–12 | Very calm market, options cheap | | 12–20 | Normal range | | 20–35 | Elevated — some nervousness | | 35–50 | High — significant event or uncertainty | | Above 50 | Extreme — panic or major event like budget, elections |

A Common Beginner Mistake with IV

Many people look at low IV and think: "Nothing is going to happen."

Wrong. Low IV just means the market currently isn't expecting much. Markets can be blindsided by news, global events, or sudden institutional moves. Low IV tells you what people are pricing in — not what will actually happen.

VIX — The Fear Gauge for the Whole Market

India VIX (Volatility Index) is closely related to IV but measures something slightly different:

India VIX measures expected volatility for the broad market (Nifty) as a whole.

While IV is specific to individual stocks or options contracts, VIX gives you a single number that represents overall market anxiety.

The simplest way to think about VIX: it's a mood reading for the entire market.

VIX in Practice

| VIX Level | Market Mood | |---|---| | Below 12 | Very calm, traders relaxed | | 12–16 | Normal conditions | | 16–20 | Some nervousness, watch carefully | | 20–25 | Elevated fear — things are uncertain | | Above 30 | Panic zone — major event or crisis |

These are not strict rules. VIX behaved very differently during COVID, during the 2024 elections, and during normal weeks. Always look at context.

IV vs VIX — What's the Difference?

A question beginners always ask. Simple answer:

IV = the volatility expectation for one specific option or stock

VIX = the volatility expectation for the entire Nifty market

Using an analogy: if India VIX is the weather forecast for the whole country, then IV for a specific stock is the forecast for your city. They're related, but not identical.

During calm markets, both tend to be low. During crises, both spike — but individual stocks can have very different IV depending on their own news or events.

Spot Price vs. Futures Price — Two Prices for the Same Thing?

Yes, sort of — and here's why this confuses beginners.

Spot price is simple: it's what Nifty or a stock is trading at right now, in real time. When you hear "Nifty is at 24,350" — that's the spot price.

Futures price is the price for agreeing to buy or sell something at a future date. If Nifty Futures for the current month are trading at 24,430, that's the price the market has agreed on for settling the contract on expiry day.

Why Are They Usually Different?

Futures prices typically trade slightly above spot — this is called "futures premium" or contango. The premium exists because:

  • There's a time component (you're locking in a price for future delivery)
  • Carrying costs (interest, dividend expectations)
  • Market sentiment about what might happen before expiry

When futures are below spot, it's called backwardation — this is less common and often signals bearish near-term sentiment.

For practical intraday purposes: if you see Nifty Futures significantly above spot, it often indicates the market expects an upward move. If futures are at a discount to spot, the market may be pricing in caution.

Max Pain — The Level Options Sellers Would Love Expiry At

Max Pain is one of those concepts that sounds mysterious but is actually quite logical once you understand who makes money in options.

Here's the key insight first: most options expire worthless. Studies consistently show that a very high percentage of options contracts — somewhere in the range of 70–80% — expire with no value. The buyers lose their premium. The sellers keep it.

So at expiry, sellers collectively win when price settles at a level that makes the maximum number of option contracts expire worthless.

That level is called Max Pain.

A Simple Example

Hypothetically, imagine Nifty is at 24,000 and approaching expiry:

  • Thousands of traders bought Calls at 24,500 and above (they need Nifty above those levels to profit)
  • Thousands of traders bought Puts at 23,500 and below (they need Nifty below those levels to profit)

If Nifty expires right around 24,000: - All the Calls above 24,000 expire worthless - All the Puts below 24,000 expire worthless - The maximum number of option buyers lose money - Option sellers collect the maximum premium

That 24,000 level would be approximately the Max Pain point.

How to Use This Practically

Max Pain is most relevant as expiry approaches — especially in the last 2–3 days before weekly or monthly expiry. Some traders observe that markets tend to drift toward the Max Pain level as expiry nears.

Important caveat: This doesn't always happen, and it's not a reliable trading signal on its own. Strong trending moves or major news can easily push price far away from Max Pain. Think of it as a gravitational pull that sometimes matters near expiry — not a magnet.

Most trading platforms (Sensibull, NSE website, option analytics tools) show the current Max Pain level automatically so you don't need to calculate it manually.

Putting It All Together — How Traders Use These Tools

No professional trader looks at just one of these numbers. They read them together, the way a doctor reads multiple vitals before making a diagnosis.

Here's a practical example of how the thinking might work:

Nifty is at 24,200. India VIX is at 13 (calm). PCR is at 1.2 (mild bullishness). Heavy Call OI sits at 24,500. Futures are trading at a slight premium to spot. Max Pain is at 24,100.

A trader reading this picture might think: "Market is calm, modestly bullish bias, but faces resistance at 24,500. If we stay below 24,500 into expiry, option sellers are in control. VIX is low so I'm not getting cheap panic-buying opportunities."

This doesn't tell you exactly what to do. But it gives you context — which is what these tools are actually for.

What Beginners Usually Get Wrong

"High OI means I should buy there." OI tells you activity, not direction. High OI means it's a significant level — not that price will move toward or away from it.

"High IV means I should sell options." High IV does make options more expensive (theoretically better for sellers). But high IV usually comes with high uncertainty — the same events that spike IV can cause sharp, unpredictable moves that destroy poorly managed short option positions.

"PCR above 1 means I should buy." PCR is a sentiment gauge, not a buy signal. It gives you the market's lean — and at extremes, that lean can reverse. Use it as context, not as a trigger.

"Max Pain means Nifty will close exactly there." Max Pain describes where option sellers benefit most. It doesn't guarantee anything. Markets ignore Max Pain regularly, especially when strong trends or news override it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Where can I see these numbers for Nifty? The NSE website (nseindia.com) has a free option chain with OI data. Platforms like Sensibull, Opstra, and TradingView offer better visualizations. India VIX is displayed on NSE and most broking platforms in real time.

Q: Is a PCR above 1 always bullish? Generally yes, but context matters. During panics or extreme events, PCR can spike to 1.5+ even as the market is falling. Always read PCR alongside price action and VIX.

Q: Can a beginner actually use all this to trade? These indicators are useful for understanding market conditions, not for generating direct buy/sell signals. A beginner is better off using them to avoid bad conditions (e.g., very high VIX = don't buy cheap-looking options, they're not cheap) rather than trying to trade off them mechanically.

Q: Why does IV drop after a major event like Budget or RBI policy? This is called IV crush. Before the event, traders buy options expecting a big move, which drives up IV. After the event — regardless of the outcome — the uncertainty is resolved. Demand for options drops. IV falls sharply. Traders who bought options just before the event often find the options lose value even if the market moved in their direction.

Q: How often is Max Pain "right"? It works some of the time and fails other times. Statistically, there's some evidence that markets tend to hover near Max Pain in the final days of expiry under normal conditions. But it's not reliable enough to trade mechanically. Treat it as one data point.
