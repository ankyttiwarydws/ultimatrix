The Evening Is Where Preparation Actually Happens

Most beginners spend their energy during market hours — watching prices tick up and down, second-guessing entries, checking their phone every few minutes. The problem is that during market hours, you are reacting. And reacting under pressure, with real money on the line, is where most mistakes happen.

Professional traders do the bulk of their thinking after the market closes. Between 3:30 PM and 9 PM, the market cannot hurt you. There are no prices moving, no decisions to make in real time. This is the window where you can look at what happened today, understand why, form a view about tomorrow, and prepare your plan — calmly, without a ticking clock.

This article gives you a simple evening routine. The first few times you do it, it might take forty-five minutes. After a couple of weeks, it will take fifteen to twenty. After a month, you will do it almost automatically.

Let's go through it step by step.

Before We Start: What You Are Actually Trying to Answer

The whole point of the evening routine is to answer one question clearly:

Tomorrow morning, when the Indian market opens, will conditions broadly favour buyers or sellers?

You are not trying to predict the exact number Nifty will hit. You are not trying to pick every stock that will move. You are forming a general morning bias — a starting expectation that shapes how cautiously or confidently you approach tomorrow's trading session.

There are really only three possible answers: - Leaning bullish — more signals favour buyers than sellers. Look for buy setups. - Leaning bearish — more signals favour sellers. Be cautious, reduce position sizes, consider sitting out. - Wait and watch — signals are mixed or unclear. Do not force anything. Let the market open and show you what it wants to do before committing.

"Wait and watch" is a completely valid and often correct answer. Beginners feel like they need to have a strong view every single day. They don't. Experienced traders will tell you that some of their best trading days are the ones where they looked at the morning picture, decided it was unclear, and did nothing.

Right — let's build the routine.

Step 1: Look at What Nifty Actually Did Today (10 minutes)

Open TradingView or your charting platform. Pull up the Nifty 50 daily chart. Look at today's candle — the bar or candle that represents today's full day of trading.

Ask yourself four things:

Did Nifty close higher or lower than yesterday? This is the most basic read. Up day or down day?

How far did it move? A 0.2% move in either direction is a quiet, indecisive day. A 1.5% move in either direction is a strong, directional day. The size of the move tells you how much conviction there was behind it.

Where did it close relative to the day's range? If Nifty fell during the day but recovered strongly to close near the top of its range — even if the close was slightly lower than yesterday — that is actually a sign of buying strength. If it opened high and sold off to close near the day's lows, that is a sign of selling pressure. The close within the day's range often tells you more than the close relative to yesterday.

Is there an obvious pattern forming on the daily chart? You do not need to be an expert chartist for this. Look at the last ten to fifteen daily candles. Is Nifty in a clear uptrend — making higher highs and higher lows each few days? Is it in a downtrend — lower highs, lower lows? Or is it chopping sideways in a range, going nowhere?

A market in a clear uptrend will often bounce from support. A market in a downtrend will often fail at resistance. A sideways market is the hardest to trade and the easiest place to get chopped up taking trades in both directions.

Write down a one-sentence summary: "Nifty closed up 0.6%, formed a strong candle, sitting near recent highs in an uptrend" or "Nifty sold off from morning highs, closed near the day's lows, trend looking weak."

Step 2: Check Which Sectors Did Well and Which Didn't (5 minutes)

The Indian market is not one homogenous thing. It is made up of different sectors — banking, IT, pharma, auto, FMCG, metals, real estate, and others. On any given day, some sectors go up while others go down. This rotation of leadership tells you a lot.

What is sector rotation? Think of the market's total money as a limited amount of water. When fund managers are feeling optimistic about banks, money flows into banking stocks — the banking sector's level rises. If they then get nervous about banks and shift to pharma, money flows out of banking and into pharma. The overall market might barely move, but within it, there is a constant shifting of which sectors are in favour.

Watching sector performance tells you two things:

First, it tells you whether today's move was broad or narrow. If Nifty went up 1% but only banking stocks moved while everything else was flat or down, the rally is narrow and potentially fragile. If all sectors moved up together, the rally has broad participation and is more convincing.

Second, it tells you which areas are attracting money right now — which is useful if you are looking for swing trade candidates.

Where to check sector performance: On NSE India's website, look for "Sector Dashboard" or "NSE Indices" which shows how each sectoral index performed. Moneycontrol also shows a sector heatmap that is quick to scan visually. TradingView lets you build a watchlist of sector indices — NIFTY BANK, NIFTY IT, NIFTY PHARMA, NIFTY AUTO, etc. — and see them all at once.

You are not trying to deeply analyse every sector. A thirty-second scan is enough: which sectors were green, which were red, was the move broad or narrow?

Step 3: Check the FII and DII Data (3 minutes)

You know what this is from Part 3. Go to NSE India's website or Moneycontrol and pull up today's FII/DII net figures.

Write down the numbers: - FII net: buyer or seller, and roughly how much - DII net: buyer or seller, and roughly how much

Then ask: do these numbers match what you saw in the chart? If Nifty had a strong up day and FIIs were net buyers of ₹3,000 crore, that is consistent — institutional money was behind the move. If Nifty had a strong up day but FIIs were net sellers of ₹2,000 crore and the rally was driven entirely by DII buying, that is a slightly different picture — it is not a negative sign, but it tells you something about who was driving the market.

The combination of chart behaviour and institutional flows is more informative than either one alone.

Step 4: Check What Global Markets Are Doing (5 minutes)

By the time the Indian market closes at 3:30 PM, some global signals are already available. European markets — London, Frankfurt, Paris — are still in the middle of their trading session. Asian markets — Tokyo, Hong Kong — have already closed for the day. US markets open around 7 PM India time, so they are not yet trading.

Pull up TradingView and quickly check: - How did Asian markets close today? (Nikkei, Hang Seng) - What are European markets doing right now? (not critical, but gives a sense of global mood) - What is crude oil doing? Up, down, roughly flat? - What is the Dollar-Rupee doing?

For the US market, you obviously cannot see tonight's result yet — but you can look at US futures. These are similar to GIFT Nifty in that they trade before and after regular US market hours. S&P 500 futures ("ES1!" on TradingView or "SPX futures" on most financial sites) give you an early read on how US markets might open this evening.

If Asian markets closed down and US futures are also negative, the probability of a difficult global backdrop increases for tomorrow. If everything is green, the global mood is supportive.

You will not have the complete picture tonight — the US market result comes in after you are likely asleep. This is why the morning routine in Part 6 is equally important. But you can get enough of a read in the evening to form a preliminary view.

Step 5: Check Tomorrow's Economic Calendar (2 minutes)

This is the step most beginners skip completely, and it is genuinely important.

Every week there are scheduled events that can move the market significantly — RBI policy meetings, GDP data releases, inflation data (CPI), US Federal Reserve announcements, major company earnings results. These events are predictable. You know the dates in advance. The failure to know they are coming is entirely avoidable.

The evening before a major scheduled event, your standard morning analysis is less reliable. The market might open calmly — GIFT Nifty might be flat, global markets might be neutral — and then completely change direction the moment the data is released at 10 AM or 12 PM.

Where to find the economic calendar: Investing.com has one of the best free economic calendars. Filter it for "India" and check what is scheduled for tomorrow. Also check for major US events — a US inflation report or Federal Reserve statement released in the evening India time can dramatically affect the next morning's mood.

Upcoming quarterly earnings of major Nifty heavyweights — Reliance, HDFC Bank, Infosys, TCS — are equally important. These are published on company websites and aggregated on NSE India's website under "Corporate Actions."

If something significant is scheduled for tomorrow, your morning bias should explicitly include: "event risk today — reduce position size and wait for the announcement before making major decisions."

Step 6: Look at Your Watchlist (5 minutes)

Now, after understanding the market context, look at the individual stocks you are tracking.

A watchlist is a list of twenty to thirty stocks you follow regularly — companies you are familiar with, whose charts you have been watching for weeks. If you do not have one yet, start building one: a mix of large-cap stocks across different sectors. Reliance, HDFC Bank, Infosys, Tata Motors, Bajaj Finance, Asian Paints, Sun Pharma — something like this is a reasonable starting point.

Look at each stock's daily chart quickly. You are asking: is this stock building up for a potential move? Is it near a support or resistance level? Is it following the market trend or diverging from it?

You are not trying to find a trade every evening. Most evenings, most stocks on your watchlist will be in the middle of their ranges — nothing particularly interesting. That is fine. You are looking for the two or three stocks that have something specific happening: a breakout setting up, a pullback to an important level, a chart pattern worth watching.

Mark those stocks separately. Tomorrow morning, these are the ones you will focus on.

Step 7: Write Your Notes and Morning Bias

This is the step that separates traders who improve from those who stay stuck.

After going through all the above, open a notebook — physical or digital, whichever you will actually use — and write a short summary. It does not need to be long. Four to six sentences is enough:

"Thursday evening. Nifty closed up 0.4%, formed a reasonable candle but on lower volume than recent sessions. FIIs were net sellers of ₹1,200 crore, DIIs bought ₹900 crore — small net outflow. Sector-wise, IT was strong, metals weak. US futures slightly negative. No major events scheduled tomorrow. Morning bias: cautiously neutral — not a strong setup in either direction. Will watch for the first thirty minutes tomorrow before taking any position. Tata Elxsi near a breakout level — watching closely."

That is your entire evening output. One paragraph. It took you roughly twenty-five minutes to get to it, but now you have a clear, calm view of tomorrow rather than waking up and trying to absorb everything in five chaotic minutes before 9:15 AM.

What a Real Evening Looks Like — Priya's Thursday

Priya is a UX designer in Hyderabad. She does swing trading in the evenings and usually takes two to three positions per month. Here is what a typical Thursday evening looks like.

It is 4:15 PM. Her work meetings are done. She opens her laptop with a cup of chai.

She pulls up Nifty on TradingView first. Today was a mixed day — Nifty opened flat, sold off a little around noon (she noticed some news about FII selling on her phone during lunch), then recovered into close to end up 0.1%. She notes the candle: small body, long lower wick. Basically a recovery candle after a mid-day dip.

She checks the sector dashboard. IT was the strongest sector today — up 1.2% — driven by some positive comments from US tech companies that came out last night. Auto was weak. Banking was flat. The rally was narrow — mostly IT.

FII/DII: FIIs sold ₹2,400 crore, DIIs bought ₹1,800 crore. Net outflow of ₹600 crore. Not a big deal, but foreign money was leaving today.

Global check: Asian markets closed mixed. US futures are slightly positive — up about 0.2%. Crude oil is flat. Dollar-Rupee is stable.

Economic calendar: nothing major tomorrow for India. But she notices the US CPI (inflation) data is out tomorrow night India time — that won't affect tomorrow's Indian session directly, but the evening after tomorrow might be interesting.

She looks at her watchlist. Most stocks are in the middle of their ranges. But she notices that Persistent Systems — an IT stock she has been watching — is sitting right at its 50-day moving average after a three-day pullback. With the IT sector strong today, this could be setting up for a bounce entry.

She writes her notes: "Thursday. Mixed day, Nifty barely moved. IT strong. FIIs mildly selling. US slightly positive. No events tomorrow. Morning bias: neutral, slightly positive for IT. Persistent Systems worth watching at open — if it holds the 50 EMA and market opens stable, possible entry."

She closes her laptop. Twenty-two minutes total. She will not think about the market again until tomorrow morning.

That is the goal.

Common Mistakes in Evening Analysis

Doing it while distracted. If you are half-watching TV or messaging on the phone while doing your evening check, you will miss things and the analysis will be shallow. Twenty focused minutes is worth more than an hour of distracted scrolling.

Overanalysing until you have too many trade ideas. If you end the evening with ten stocks you are "considering," you have too many. Narrow it down to two maximum. More than that and you will either overwhelm yourself tomorrow morning or spread your capital too thin.

Changing your bias based on something you read on Twitter or Telegram at 10 PM. You did your analysis. You formed a view. Then someone in a trading group says "tomorrow will be a bloodbath" and you start doubting yourself. Unless you see a genuinely new piece of information — a major company announcement, a global event — stick with the analysis you did. Random opinions from unknown people on the internet should not undo twenty minutes of structured thinking.

Skipping it when you are tired. The evenings when you feel least like doing the analysis are often the most important ones — usually after a bad trading day when your judgement is most vulnerable. On those evenings, the routine is especially important. Even a ten-minute version is better than nothing.
