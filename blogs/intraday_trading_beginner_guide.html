Why Most Beginners Lose Money in the First Month

Let's be honest for a second.

You've probably seen someone on Instagram or YouTube showing their trading profits. Big green numbers. Screenshots of gains. "I made ₹15,000 in 2 hours from my phone."

So you open a Zerodha or Groww account, put in some money, and try it yourself.

And then something strange happens.

The market does the exact opposite of what you expected. You buy, it falls. You sell, it shoots up. You wait patiently, it does nothing. You get impatient and enter, it reverses immediately.

Sound familiar?

This is not because you're unlucky or bad at math. It's because nobody taught you how price actually moves — and more importantly, why.

This article is written for people who are genuinely curious about intraday trading — not to make it sound easy, but to give you an honest, practical foundation before you risk real money.

First Things First: What Is Intraday Trading?

Intraday trading means you buy and sell within the same day. You don't hold positions overnight. Everything is squared off before the market closes at 3:30 PM.

The appeal is obvious: markets move every day. Even if Reliance Industries doesn't move much in a year, within a single day it might go up ₹30 and come back down ₹20. Intraday traders try to catch those moves.

But here's the catch — and this is something most beginners don't realize until they've lost money:

Catching small daily moves is one of the hardest things to do consistently in financial markets.

It requires fast thinking, emotional control, a clear system, and the ability to do nothing when the market gives you no good opportunity. That last part — doing nothing — is often the hardest.

Understanding Candlestick Charts (Without the Jargon)

If you've ever looked at a stock chart and felt confused by all those colored bars, you're not alone. Let's break it down simply.

Each candlestick represents one unit of time — could be 1 minute, 5 minutes, 15 minutes, or a day — and it tells you four things:

  • Where the price opened when that time period started
  • Where it closed when the period ended
  • The highest point it reached in between
  • The lowest point it fell to in between

A green (or white) candle means the price closed higher than it opened — buyers were in control during that period.

A red (or black) candle means the price closed lower than it opened — sellers dominated.

What Beginners Get Wrong About Candles

Most beginners look at a big green candle and immediately think: "Price is going up, I should buy!"

But a single candle tells you almost nothing on its own.

What matters more is what comes after.

Imagine this: A stock shoots up sharply with a big green candle. Then the next three candles are tiny, barely moving, with long thin lines sticking out above (called wicks). That's the market hesitating. Buyers pushed hard, but couldn't keep going. This often means the move is running out of energy.

Contrast that with a strong uptrend where candles are consistently green, bodies are large, wicks are small, and price keeps making new highs. That's genuine momentum.

The story isn't in one candle. It's in the sequence.

Market Structure: The Simplest and Most Underrated Concept

Here's something that more experienced traders use every day but rarely talk about in beginner content.

Markets move in patterns. Not magical patterns — just simple ones:

In an uptrend: Price keeps making higher highs and higher lows. Think of it like climbing stairs. Each step up is higher than the last. Each time it pulls back, it stops higher than the last pullback.

In a downtrend: Price keeps making lower highs and lower lows. Like falling down stairs. Every bounce is weaker than the one before.

In a sideways market: Price bounces back and forth between two rough levels — a ceiling (resistance) and a floor (support). It doesn't go anywhere.

Why This Matters Practically

If you look at a chart and see price making higher highs and higher lows, you have a basic uptrend. In that situation, looking for opportunities to buy on small pullbacks makes more sense than trying to short it.

If you see it moving sideways and can't figure out the direction — that's actually useful information too. It tells you: this might not be a good time to trade.

Many beginners waste money trying to trade sideways markets because they look "safe" when they're actually a trap. Price chops back and forth, triggering stop-losses in both directions.

VWAP — The One Indicator Worth Learning Early

Most indicators are just mathematical formulas applied to past price. VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price) is a little different, and it's genuinely useful for intraday trading.

Here's the simple version of what VWAP is:

Imagine every trade that happened today. Some happened with 100 shares, some with 10,000 shares. VWAP calculates the average price of all those trades, weighted by how many shares were traded at each price.

Why does that matter?

Because large institutional traders — mutual funds, hedge funds, big proprietary desks — often use VWAP as a benchmark. They try to buy close to or below VWAP, and sell close to or above it.

So when price is above VWAP, it often means the day's buyers are in profit. Sentiment tends to be more positive. Retail traders, big funds — they're collectively sitting on gains for the day.

When price is below VWAP, the opposite is true. The average buyer for the day is losing money. Sellers tend to have more control.

How to Actually Use It

Think of VWAP not as a signal, but as context.

If you're thinking about buying, and price is above a rising VWAP with clean momentum — that's a better environment than buying when price is chopping back and forth around a flat VWAP line.

One classic mistake: a beginner sees price touch VWAP and immediately buys "the bounce." But VWAP support only tends to hold when the overall trend is still intact. Context always matters more than the level itself.

How Price Actually Moves During the Day

One thing that's hard to explain until you've watched live charts for a while: intraday price movement is often messy.

It's not clean arrows going up and coming down like a textbook chart. It's:

  • A sharp move up for 10 minutes
  • Then 20 minutes of going nowhere, tiny candles
  • Then a random spike down that looks scary
  • Then slowly grinding back up
  • Then a big drop right before lunch

Understanding this rhythm helps enormously.

The Indian market has some fairly predictable patterns in terms of when things happen:

9:15–9:45 AM (Opening Session) This is the most volatile part of the day. Overnight news, global markets, F&O expiry — everything hits at once. Gaps up or down are common. For beginners, this period is often better to observe than to trade. Experienced traders call it "amateur hour" because it can move irrationally.

10:00–11:30 AM Markets tend to find a direction. This is often the best window for clean intraday moves. Momentum is cleaner, volume is active, and institutional activity picks up.

12:00–1:30 PM (Lunch Lull) Activity drops. Volume dries up. Price often drifts sideways or makes small random moves. Many experienced traders avoid this window entirely.

2:00–3:30 PM Volume picks up again. Positions are being squared off. This can create sharp moves — both real and false. Last 15 minutes especially can be erratic.

The Concept Nobody Talks About: Momentum vs. Noise

Here's one of the most honest things you'll read about intraday trading:

Most of what the market does every day is noise.

That tiny movement up and down every few seconds? Most of it is random. It's not a signal. It's just the natural back-and-forth of buyers and sellers constantly adjusting.

The challenge for an intraday trader isn't finding movement — movement is everywhere. The challenge is identifying quality movement. Movement that has conviction behind it. Movement that's likely to continue for at least a few more minutes.

How do you tell the difference?

Strong momentum tends to look like: - Large candle bodies - One side clearly winning (consistent green or red) - Wicks are small, not long - Price moves away from a level without looking back immediately - Volume is elevated, not flat

Noise tends to look like: - Small overlapping candles, neither green nor red candles dominating - Long wicks in both directions (the market keeps getting rejected) - Price moves a bit, comes back, moves a bit, comes back - Volume is low and irregular

Learning to tell these apart takes time — honestly, weeks or months of chart watching. But it's the real skill of intraday trading.

The Mistake That Costs Beginners the Most Money

It's not picking the wrong stock.

It's trading during choppy conditions.

Here's a simple example to illustrate. Imagine you're trying to cross a busy road. Sometimes there's a clear gap in traffic and you can walk across easily. Sometimes it's chaotic — cars going in both directions, no clear pattern, everyone honking.

A choppy market is like the second scenario. Even if you're a good "road crosser," you're going to get hit eventually if you keep trying in chaotic conditions.

Many beginners see a choppy market and think: "I just need to figure out which direction it's going." But sometimes the honest answer is: it's not going anywhere right now, and forcing a trade here is just gambling.

The discipline to sit on your hands and wait for a clear setup is genuinely one of the most valuable skills in trading. It sounds boring. It is boring. That's the point.

Stop Losses — And Why They Don't Always Work the Way You Think

A stop loss is an order that automatically exits your trade if price moves against you by a set amount. It's your safety net.

Every beginner is told: "Always use a stop loss." Good advice.

But here's something that catches traders off guard:

A stop loss doesn't always execute at the price you set.

Why? Because of gaps and slippage.

Gaps happen when a stock opens dramatically higher or lower than where it closed the previous day. Maybe a company announced bad quarterly results after market hours. If you were holding a position overnight and set a stop at ₹500, but the stock opens at ₹460 the next morning — your stop executes at ₹460, not ₹500.

That's called gap risk. It's one reason why many experienced intraday traders avoid holding positions overnight.

Slippage is similar but happens during fast market moves. You want to exit at ₹500, but the price is moving so fast that by the time your order reaches the exchange, it executes at ₹497.

This is especially important in options trading, where liquidity can be thin in certain contracts and prices can move extremely quickly.

Scalping: Catching Small Moves Many Times a Day

You've probably heard of scalping — the idea of making many small trades throughout the day, capturing 5–10 points at a time instead of waiting for large moves.

It sounds appealing. But let's be real about what it involves.

The Math Problem

Say you're trading Nifty futures. Each lot is 50 units. You want to capture 10 points per trade.

That sounds like ₹500 per trade (10 points × 50 units).

But consider: - Brokerage, STT, exchange fees, GST — can easily eat ₹100–200 per trade - One bad trade where your stop gets hit for 15 points = ₹750 loss - Three bad trades in a row = ₹2,250 gone

For scalping to work, you need a very high win rate, lightning-fast execution, and iron discipline. Most professional scalpers also have direct market access and much lower transaction costs than retail traders.

This doesn't mean don't try it. It means: paper trade it first. See if your system actually works before putting real money in.

A More Realistic Starting Point

Rather than scalping 1-minute charts, many beginners do better starting on 15-minute charts. The moves are cleaner. There's less noise. You have more time to think.

You're not going to get rich quickly, but you're also less likely to lose everything quickly while you're learning.

Can AI Help With Trading?

Honestly — yes and no.

AI can be genuinely useful for: - Identifying market conditions (trending vs. choppy) - Classifying momentum quality - Summarizing multiple signals into a cleaner picture - Backtesting ideas quickly

What AI cannot do: - Predict what price will do next - Remove the uncertainty that is fundamental to markets - Replace the judgment that comes from experience

Think of AI as a better calculator, not a crystal ball.

There are traders building TradingView dashboards using Pine Script that give them a quick overview of conditions — something like: "Trend is bullish, VWAP is sloping up, momentum is moderate, choppiness is low." That's useful framing. It doesn't tell you the next candle direction. It tells you the environment you're operating in.

The Psychology Nobody Prepares You For

Here's something that surprises almost every new trader.

You can study everything. Learn every pattern. Read every book. And then you sit down in front of a live chart with real money, and something strange happens to your brain.

A trade goes against you by ₹200. Your stomach tightens. You think "maybe it'll come back" instead of following your stop. It drops another ₹300. Now you're telling yourself "I'll exit if it comes back to breakeven." It doesn't.

Or the opposite: you catch a perfect move, make ₹1,000, feel like a genius. Then you immediately take another trade with double the size because you're "on a roll." And give it all back.

This is not a weakness. This is human nature. Markets are specifically designed (unintentionally) to exploit our psychological hardwiring — our fear of loss, our desire for excitement, our urge to "make back" what we lost.

The traders who survive long-term aren't necessarily the smartest. They're the ones who figured out how to manage themselves as much as the market.

A Simple Framework for Beginners: Before You Enter Any Trade

Before pressing buy or sell, ask yourself these five questions:

  1. What is the overall trend today? (Uptrend, downtrend, or sideways?)
  2. Where is price relative to VWAP? (Above, below, or chopping through it?)
  3. Is momentum clean or messy? (Are candles moving clearly in one direction?)
  4. Where is my stop loss? (Before entry, not after)
  5. What is my target, and is the risk:reward reasonable? (Most traders aim for at least 1:2 — risk ₹500 to make ₹1,000)

If you can't answer all five clearly, it's a sign the setup isn't ready.

Common Beginner Mistakes (Honest List)

  • Trading the opening 15 minutes with large size — volatile, noisy, high chance of getting whipsawed
  • Moving stop losses further away when a trade goes against you ("I'll just give it more room")
  • Revenge trading — taking a poor-quality trade immediately after a loss to "make it back"
  • Trading every day regardless of conditions — some days just don't offer good setups
  • Ignoring the broader market — if Nifty is crashing, most stocks will too
  • Over-reliance on one indicator — no single tool tells the whole story
  • Trading money you cannot afford to lose — financial pressure destroys decision-making

Practical Starting Points for Absolute Beginners

If you're genuinely new to this, here's a honest progression:

Month 1–2: Watch, don't trade Open charts. Watch how Nifty moves through the day. Notice the patterns. See how VWAP works. You'll learn more from watching than any video course.

Month 3–4: Paper trade Most brokers and platforms let you practice with virtual money. Do this seriously — as if it were real money. Keep a journal of every trade: why you entered, what happened, what you learned.

Month 5 onwards: Start tiny if you trade real money Start with the smallest position size possible. The goal at this stage isn't profit. It's to learn how to manage the psychology of having real money at risk.

Final Thought: Trading Is a Skill, Not a Shortcut

India has roughly 70 lakh active F&O traders. Studies consistently show the majority lose money in any given year.

That's not meant to discourage you. It's meant to give you a realistic benchmark.

The traders who make consistent money aren't special. They just went through the same losses, the same confusion, the same "why does the market do this?!" moments — and kept learning instead of quitting or doubling down recklessly.

Trading can be intellectually fascinating and financially rewarding. But it takes time, discipline, and an honest look at your own behavior.

If you approach it that way — as a craft to be learned, not a lottery ticket — you'll already be ahead of most beginners.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How much money do I need to start intraday trading in India? You can technically start with ₹5,000–10,000, but this limits your options and doesn't leave much room for losses. Many experienced traders suggest having at least ₹50,000–1,00,000 dedicated capital before taking intraday trading seriously — and only money you can afford to lose.

Q: Is intraday trading good for beginners? Honestly, intraday trading is one of the harder forms of trading to learn. Swing trading (holding for days to weeks) is often easier to start with because you have more time to think and mistakes are less instant.

Q: Which stocks are best for intraday trading? Highly liquid, actively traded large-cap stocks are generally preferred — they have tighter spreads and more predictable behavior. Nifty 50 stocks are commonly used. Thinly traded small-caps can be unpredictable and dangerous for beginners.

Q: Can I use a mobile app for intraday trading? Yes, most brokers offer good mobile apps. But many traders find desktop setups better for serious intraday work — multiple screens help, and fat-finger mistakes on mobile are expensive.

Q: How do taxes work on intraday trading in India? Intraday trading profits are treated as speculative business income and taxed at your applicable income tax slab rate. Keep proper records — consult a CA familiar with trading taxation.

Q: Is there a "best time" to trade intraday? Many experienced traders focus on the 10 AM–12 PM window when trends are more established and volume is healthy. Opening and closing 15–20 minutes tend to be noisiest.
