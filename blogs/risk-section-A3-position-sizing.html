The Question Nobody Asks (But Should)

When most beginners decide to buy a stock, the question they ask is: "should I buy this stock?"

The question they almost never ask — until they have lost money for a few months — is: "how many shares should I buy?"

These seem like two separate questions, but they are actually equally important. Getting the stock right and getting the quantity wrong can still result in a loss that damages your account more than it should. Getting the quantity right means that even if the stock goes against you, the damage is controlled, planned, and survivable.

Position sizing is simply the process of calculating the correct number of shares — or lots, in options and futures — to buy for any given trade, based on your account size, your stop-loss level, and how much of your capital you are willing to risk.

It is arithmetic. There is no guesswork. Once you understand the formula, you will never again stare at a buy screen wondering "should I buy 50 shares or 200?"

The One Formula You Need

Here it is. This is the complete position sizing formula:

Number of shares = Maximum loss allowed ÷ Risk per share

Where: - Maximum loss allowed = 2% of your total trading capital (the amount from the 2% rule) - Risk per share = Your entry price minus your stop-loss price

That is it. Two inputs, one output. Let's make sure both inputs are clear before we work through examples.

Maximum loss allowed is the rupee cap on how much this single trade is allowed to cost you. If your account has ₹1,00,000, your 2% maximum loss is ₹2,000. This is the most you are willing to lose on this trade — not 2% of the share price, not 2% of the trade value. 2% of your total account.

Risk per share is how much you lose on each share if the trade hits your stop-loss. If you buy a stock at ₹500 and your stop-loss is at ₹475, your risk per share is ₹25. This number comes from your chart analysis — specifically, from where you believe the trade idea becomes invalid.

Divide the first by the second, and you have your position size.

Worked Examples at Different Account Sizes

Let's run through this with four different account sizes so you can see exactly what it looks like for your situation.

Account size: ₹50,000

2% rule: maximum loss per trade = ₹1,000

Trade example: Tata Motors Entry: ₹900 Stop-loss (based on chart support): ₹870 Risk per share: ₹900 − ₹870 = ₹30

Shares to buy: ₹1,000 ÷ ₹30 = 33 shares

Total capital deployed: 33 × ₹900 = ₹29,700 (about 59% of account — that is fine, you are not required to deploy your entire account) Maximum possible loss: 33 × ₹30 = ₹990 (approximately ₹1,000, within your limit)

Account size: ₹2,00,000

2% rule: maximum loss per trade = ₹4,000

Trade example: Bajaj Finance Entry: ₹7,200 Stop-loss: ₹7,000 Risk per share: ₹7,200 − ₹7,000 = ₹200

Shares to buy: ₹4,000 ÷ ₹200 = 20 shares

Total capital deployed: 20 × ₹7,200 = ₹1,44,000 (72% of account) Maximum possible loss: 20 × ₹200 = ₹4,000

Account size: ₹5,00,000

2% rule: maximum loss per trade = ₹10,000

Trade example: Infosys Entry: ₹1,800 Stop-loss: ₹1,750 Risk per share: ₹1,800 − ₹1,750 = ₹50

Shares to buy: ₹10,000 ÷ ₹50 = 200 shares

Total capital deployed: 200 × ₹1,800 = ₹3,60,000 (72% of account) Maximum possible loss: 200 × ₹50 = ₹10,000

Account size: ₹20,00,000

2% rule: maximum loss per trade = ₹40,000

Trade example: Reliance Industries Entry: ₹2,500 Stop-loss: ₹2,440 Risk per share: ₹2,500 − ₹2,440 = ₹60

Shares to buy: ₹40,000 ÷ ₹60 = 666 shares (round down to 660 for a clean number)

Total capital deployed: 660 × ₹2,500 = ₹16,50,000 (82% of account) Maximum possible loss: 660 × ₹60 = ₹39,600 (just under ₹40,000 limit — fine)

Notice something across all four examples: the position sizing formula automatically adjusts for everything. Bigger accounts take larger positions in rupee terms but the same percentage risk. Tighter stop-losses allow more shares. Wider stop-losses force fewer shares. The formula does this calibration for you without any subjective judgment.

The Part Where Beginners Get Confused

Two things commonly trip people up when they first try this formula.

Confusion 1: "The formula is telling me to deploy 80% of my account in one trade. Is that too much?"

Not necessarily — and here is why. The formula is controlling risk, not capital deployed. The ₹40,000 maximum loss on a ₹20L account is the controlled risk. The ₹16.5L of capital deployed is just the purchase price of those shares. If the trade works and the stock does not hit the stop-loss, you still have ₹16.5L worth of shares. Your money is not "at risk" in the sense that it might disappear — it is deployed into an asset that has value. The actual risk — the amount that could disappear if the stop is hit — is only ₹39,600. That is the number to focus on.

That said, there is a practical point here: if you are deploying 80-90% of your account in one position, you have very little flexibility to take other trades. Most traders with larger accounts keep individual positions to 20-40% of capital for this reason — not because of risk management math, but because of diversification and opportunity management. We will cover this in the portfolio risk article coming up in this section.

Confusion 2: "My stop-loss is very wide and the formula gives me only 3 shares. That seems pointless."

This is the formula working correctly — and it is giving you important information. A very wide stop-loss means your risk per share is high, which means the position size is small to keep total risk controlled.

This is not a problem with the formula. It is a signal that either: (a) The stop-loss you have chosen is too wide and you should look for a tighter, more technically valid level, or (b) The stock is simply too volatile or too expensive for your current account size, and you should find a different trade where the math works better.

Many beginners see a small position size as the formula failing. It is actually the formula protecting you from taking an oversized risk on a wide-stop trade.

Where Does the Stop-Loss Price Come From?

This is the question that position sizing cannot answer for you — and it is the most important input in the formula.

The stop-loss price should come from your chart analysis, not from a percentage. "I'll use a 5% stop-loss on everything" is not chart analysis. It is guessing. And it usually results in one of two problems: the stop is either too tight (gets hit by normal daily volatility before the trade has a chance to work) or too wide (risks too much per trade when the formula is applied).

A technically valid stop-loss is placed at a level where, if price reaches it, your original trade idea is clearly wrong. Common approaches:

Below a support level. If you are buying because a stock bounced from a support zone, your stop-loss goes just below that support. If the price breaks below support, the bounce idea is invalid.

Below the previous day's low. For a swing trade, if you are buying a stock that made a strong candle yesterday, your stop goes just below yesterday's low. If price drops below there, yesterday's strength was fake.

Below a moving average. Some traders use the 20-day or 50-day EMA as their stop reference point. If price closes below the moving average they were relying on for the trade, they exit.

The key word in all of these is "below a level that invalidates the trade idea" — not "at a random percentage below my entry."

If you are not yet confident in identifying stop-loss levels from charts, start with the next article in this section which covers stop-losses in full detail. Then come back and the position sizing formula will feel even more natural.

A Practical Habit: Write It Down Before You Trade

Here is a small but high-impact habit: before placing any trade, write these five numbers on paper or in a notes app:

  1. Entry price: ₹_____
  2. Stop-loss price: ₹_____
  3. Risk per share: ₹_____ (entry minus stop)
  4. Maximum loss allowed (2% of account): ₹_____
  5. Shares to buy: _____ (max loss ÷ risk per share)

Then add: 6. Target price: ₹_ (where you plan to exit if the trade works) 7. Potential gain if target hit: ₹___ (shares × price difference to target)

Writing these down does two things. First, it forces the calculation — you cannot skip it if it is a required step before trading. Second, it makes the trade concrete and pre-planned. You are not making decisions in real time under pressure. You made them before the market opened, calmly, with a clear head.

This piece of paper or note is your trade plan. If the market opens and the setup does not look exactly as you planned, you can refer back to it and decide whether the original plan still applies. If anything significant has changed, you can recalculate rather than guess.

When the Math Doesn't Work — Walk Away

Sometimes you will run the position sizing formula and discover that the mathematically correct number of shares to buy is so small it feels impractical — maybe 3 or 4 shares of a high-priced stock.

This is usually a sign that the trade is not the right fit for your current account size. Not every stock is appropriate for every account size. A stock trading at ₹15,000 per share with a technically valid stop-loss 200 points away has a risk per share of ₹200. On a ₹1L account with a ₹2,000 maximum loss, the formula gives you 10 shares — a total trade value of ₹1,50,000. You do not have ₹1,50,000 to deploy. The trade does not work for your account size right now.

The answer is not to widen the stop-loss to make the math work. Widening the stop to fit the formula backward is dangerous — you are no longer placing the stop where the chart says to, you are placing it where your calculator told you to. These are not the same thing, and the result is usually a poorly placed stop that fails.

The answer is to find a different stock where the position sizing math works within your account constraints. There are always other trades.

Building Your Position Sizing Into a Routine

Once you have done this calculation a dozen times, it becomes fast and automatic. Eventually you will do it in your head in about twenty seconds. But until that point, do it on paper every single time.

Traders who skip the calculation — even experienced ones — are the traders who occasionally find themselves in a trade that is much larger than they intended, under much more emotional pressure than they can handle, making panicked decisions. The calculation is not just math. It is also the moment where you consciously acknowledge what you are risking and confirm that you are comfortable with it before the trade starts.

That acknowledgement — that brief moment of rational pre-commitment — is worth more than any indicator or strategy you will ever find.
