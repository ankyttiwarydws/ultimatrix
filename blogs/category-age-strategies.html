Age-Based Strategies — Because Where You Are in Life Changes Everything

The right approach at 25 is the wrong approach at 55. Here is how to know the difference.

Most trading and investing education treats everyone the same. The 23-year-old on their first salary and the 54-year-old with thirty years of savings both get the same generic advice: diversify, invest for the long term, do not panic sell. As if the stakes, the responsibilities, and the time available are identical for both of them.

They are not. Not even close.

A 25-year-old who loses 50% of their trading capital has time, income, and compounding on their side. Recovery is painful but entirely achievable. A 54-year-old who loses 50% of their investment portfolio has six years until retirement and no salary coming in after that. Recovery requires everything to go right with no room for error. These are fundamentally different situations — and they require fundamentally different approaches.

This section follows one person across their entire financial life — from a first job at ₹4–5 LPA in their early 20s, through the responsibilities of the 30s and 40s, into the peak earning years of the 50s, and finally into retirement. The happy version, where things go reasonably well. Because before you can plan for the difficult scenarios, you need to understand what good decision-making looks like at each stage when things are going right.

Each article gives you real salary numbers, real expense breakdowns for both metro and mid-sized city living, real allocation frameworks, and honest guidance about what the right approach actually is at each stage — not the approach that sounds good in theory, but the one that fits the actual life of someone in their 30s with an EMI and ageing parents, or someone in their 50s who has built something real and cannot afford to lose it.

If you are in your 20s, the compounding argument in this section will probably change how urgently you start your SIP. If you are in your 40s or 50s, the recovery time maths will probably change how you think about position sizing. And if you are at or near retirement, the framework here is honest in a way that most financial content is not about what should and should not go into a trading account at that stage of life.
