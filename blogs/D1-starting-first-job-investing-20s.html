The Moment Everyone Gets Wrong

You have just landed your first job. Salary is credited for the first time and there is a number sitting in your bank account that feels real in a way your pocket money never did.

And almost immediately, someone — a friend, a YouTube video, a cousin — tells you: you should be investing in the stock market.

They are not wrong. Your 20s genuinely are the best time to start. But there is a big difference between investing and trading, a big difference between starting right and starting fast, and a very expensive difference between using money you can afford to lose and money you absolutely cannot.

This article is for someone earning their first salary — somewhere between ₹25,000 and ₹42,000 per month in hand — figuring out what to actually do with it. Not theory. Not jargon. Real numbers, real decisions, in the right order.

First, Let's See Where the Money Actually Goes

Let's take two versions of the same person. Both 22 years old, both earning ₹4.5 LPA — which works out to roughly ₹35,000–₹37,000 per month in hand after tax and PF deductions.

Person A lives in Mumbai or Bengaluru.

Rent for a decent shared flat (2–3 people): ₹10,000–₹12,000 per month as their share. Food — cooking at home most days, eating out occasionally: ₹5,000–₹6,000. Transport — metro, auto, or fuel: ₹3,000–₹4,000. Phone, internet, subscriptions: ₹1,500. Miscellaneous — clothes, toiletries, a movie, a bad day where you order food twice: ₹3,000–₹4,000.

Total expenses: roughly ₹23,000–₹27,000 per month.

That leaves ₹8,000–₹12,000 per month to actually do something with.

Person B lives in Indore, Nagpur, Jaipur, or a similar mid-sized city.

Rent: ₹5,000–₹7,000 (shared or sometimes alone). Food: ₹4,000–₹5,000. Transport: ₹1,500–₹2,000. Phone and internet: ₹1,000. Miscellaneous: ₹2,500.

Total expenses: roughly ₹14,000–₹18,000 per month.

That leaves ₹17,000–₹21,000 per month.

The point of this exercise is not to make you feel envious or lucky depending on where you live. It is to show you that the person in Mumbai with ₹10,000 left over and the person in Indore with ₹20,000 left over are in genuinely different situations — and the decisions they make with that money should be proportional, not identical.

Before Anything Else: The Emergency Fund

Before investing. Before trading. Before a SIP. Before anything.

You need an emergency fund.

An emergency fund is simply money that sits in a savings account or liquid fund — not the stock market, not fixed deposits with lock-ins — that you can access within 24 hours if something goes wrong. Job loss. Medical emergency. A sudden flight home. Your laptop dying when you need it for work.

How much? Three to six months of your total expenses.

If your monthly expenses are ₹25,000, your emergency fund should be ₹75,000 to ₹1,50,000. If your expenses are ₹15,000, it is ₹45,000 to ₹90,000.

This sounds like a lot when you are just starting. It is. That is fine — you build it over time.

Why does this come first?

Because without an emergency fund, your investments become your emergency fund — whether you want them to be or not. The market has a way of going down at exactly the moment life gets difficult. If you have invested ₹50,000 and suddenly need ₹30,000 for something urgent, you will be forced to sell your investments at whatever price the market is offering that day. Which might be a loss.

The emergency fund protects your investments from you — from the version of you that is stressed, scared, and needs money right now.

Where to keep it: A high-interest savings account, or a liquid mutual fund. Not in a locker. Not in a fixed deposit that takes 7 working days to break. Somewhere genuinely liquid.

Now, the SIP — And Why Starting at 22 Is Worth So Much More Than Starting at 32

Once your emergency fund is in place — or even while you are building it — you should start a SIP. Even a small one.

SIP stands for Systematic Investment Plan. It is just a way of investing a fixed amount into a mutual fund every month automatically. You set it up once, and on a chosen date each month, the money moves from your bank account into the mutual fund. No decision-making. No timing the market. Just consistent, regular investing.

Now here is the number that will probably surprise you.

Imagine two people. Both eventually invest the same amount of money in the same type of mutual fund earning the same average return of 12% per year.

Person A starts a SIP of ₹3,000 per month at age 22 and invests for 10 years (age 22 to 32). Then they stop completely — not a single rupee more. They let the money sit and grow until they are 60.

Person B starts a SIP of ₹3,000 per month at age 32 and invests every single month without stopping until they are 60. That is 28 years of continuous investing.

Person A invested for 10 years. Person B invested for 28 years.

Who has more money at 60?

Person A has approximately ₹3.8 crore. Person B has approximately ₹3.2 crore.

The person who stopped investing at 32 ends up with more money than the person who invested continuously from 32 to 60 — just because they started 10 years earlier.

This is compounding. Your money earns returns. Those returns earn returns. The longer this goes on, the more powerful it becomes. And it rewards early starters dramatically.

The practical version: even ₹1,000 or ₹2,000 per month started at 22 is worth more than ₹5,000 per month started at 30. Not because of the amount — but because of the time.

How much to put in a SIP? As a starting point, aim for at least 20% of what you have left after expenses. For the Mumbai person with ₹10,000 surplus, that is ₹2,000 per month. For the Indore person with ₹20,000 surplus, that is ₹4,000. These are not rules — they are starting points. As your salary grows, your SIP amount should grow with it.

What kind of mutual fund? For a 22-year-old with no dependents, a simple Nifty 50 index fund or a flexicap fund is a perfectly fine starting point. This is not the place to chase exotic schemes or guaranteed-return promises from social media.

Now — Trading. What It Actually Is, and What It Costs to Learn

Let's talk about trading — because that is probably part of why you are on this website.

Trading is different from investing. Investing, like the SIP above, is patient and long-term. Trading is actively buying and selling stocks (or futures, options, currencies) to try to profit from short-term price movements. It requires time, attention, skill, and — honestly — a fair amount of experience before it becomes consistently profitable.

Here is something that most beginner trading content does not say directly: most people who start trading lose money in their first year or two. Not because they are stupid. Because trading is a skill, and like any skill, there is a learning curve. And the learning curve in trading has a financial cost.

This does not mean you should not start. It means you should start the right way — with money you have decided upfront you can afford to lose completely, and which will not damage your life if you do.

The 5–10% rule of learning capital

A healthy starting point is this: do not allocate more than 5–10% of your monthly savings to trading or learning to trade. Not 5% of your salary — 5% of what you are actually saving after expenses and SIP.

Let's put numbers to it.

Mumbai person: ₹10,000 surplus, ₹2,000 going to SIP, ₹8,000 remaining savings. 10% of ₹8,000 is ₹800. That is learning capital for trading.

That sounds tiny. It is. That is the point.

Indore person: ₹20,000 surplus, ₹4,000 going to SIP, ₹16,000 remaining savings. 10% of ₹16,000 is ₹1,600.

With ₹800–₹1,600 a month, you are not going to get rich from trading. But you are going to learn real lessons with real stakes — which is far more valuable than paper trading (simulated trading with fake money), and far less dangerous than going in with ₹20,000 right away.

Why not more? Because at this stage of life, what you are doing is paying tuition. Think of these losses — and there will be losses — as the cost of learning a skill. A coaching class for a skill like CA or UPSC can cost ₹50,000–₹1,00,000. Trading teaches you differently — it charges you gradually, trade by trade, mistake by mistake. That is fine, as long as the mistakes are sized for learning, not for survival.

The Order of Operations — Put This on Your Phone

If you take nothing else from this article, take this sequence:

Step 1: Know your actual monthly numbers. Income in hand, fixed expenses, what is left.

Step 2: Build your emergency fund first. Start putting a portion of your surplus toward it every month until you have three months of expenses saved.

Step 3: Start a SIP. Even ₹1,000 or ₹2,000. The amount matters less than the habit — and the timing.

Step 4: Once your emergency fund is in shape and your SIP is running, you can explore trading with a small portion of remaining savings. Treat it as education money.

Step 5: As your salary grows — and in your 20s it should — grow your SIP proportionally before you grow your trading capital. The compounding argument only works if you keep feeding it.

That is the whole framework. It is not complicated. It is just easy to skip steps when something exciting is in front of you — like a stock that everyone says is going to double.

A Few Things Worth Saying Directly

Do not use your emergency fund to trade. This sounds obvious. It is not. When a trade looks very attractive and your savings account is right there, the temptation is real. The emergency fund is not trading capital. It is protection from life.

Do not use credit cards or borrow money to trade. Ever. Not even once, not even for a sure thing. There is no sure thing.

The goal for your first year is not to make money. It is to understand how markets work, how your emotions behave when real money is involved, and to avoid the large, expensive mistakes that most beginners make. If you end the year having lost less than ₹10,000 and having learned how markets move — that is a good year.

Your salary is your biggest asset right now. Not your stock portfolio. Not your trading account. Your ability to earn ₹4–5 LPA — and to grow that over time — is the engine that funds everything else. Protect it. Do not let trading anxiety affect your work.

What This Looks Like at the End of the Year

Let's close with a picture. You are 22. You are earning ₹4.5 LPA. You live in a mid-sized city. You follow the steps above for one year.

By the end of that year, you have:

  • An emergency fund of ₹40,000–₹60,000 (you have been building it steadily)
  • A mutual fund SIP running since month two or three, now with ₹18,000–₹30,000 invested
  • A trading account where you have put in a small amount, made some trades, probably lost a little, learned a lot
  • Zero debt related to trading
  • Your salary intact and growing

This is not a dramatic story. It does not have big wins or hair-raising stories. But it is the foundation that the person in their 30s and 40s looks back on and is grateful for — because they did not blow their early savings on expensive mistakes.

The market is not going anywhere. It will be there next month, next year, and in ten years. You have time to learn it properly.

Try This This Weekend

Sit down with your last month's bank statement. Not to feel guilty about anything — just to see the real numbers.

Write down: income in hand, what you spent, what was left. Most people have never actually done this and have only a rough sense of where their money goes.

Then ask yourself honestly: do I have three months of expenses saved somewhere I can access immediately? If not, that is your starting point — before anything else.

Once you know your real numbers, the rest of the decisions become much easier to make.
