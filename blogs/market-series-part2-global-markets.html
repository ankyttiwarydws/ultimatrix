A Question Worth Asking

Why should you care about what happened in America last night?

You live in India. You are trading Indian stocks. The Tata Motors factory is in Pune, not New York. HDFC Bank's customers are in Mumbai and Chennai, not San Francisco. So why do Indian traders wake up every morning and immediately check what the US market did overnight?

The answer has nothing to do with geography. It has everything to do with money — specifically, where the world's largest pools of money decide to go each day.

Once you understand this, a lot of confusing things will start to make sense. Days when the Indian market falls for no obvious reason. Days when everything in India looks fine but Nifty still opens lower. Days when great Indian company news barely moves the market because something scary happened abroad.

Let's build this up from the beginning.

The Global Money Pool

Imagine the world's investment money as one enormous water tank. That tank is fed by pension funds, insurance companies, banks, and large investment firms from the US, Europe, Japan, and elsewhere. Every day, the managers of this money decide where to pour it — which countries, which sectors, which assets.

India gets a portion of that water every time foreign investors decide Indian stocks look attractive. When they pour money into India, more buyers show up in our market and prices go up. When they decide to pull money out — maybe because America looks more attractive, or because something scared them — they sell Indian stocks and take the money elsewhere. More sellers, fewer buyers. Prices fall.

This is why what happens in New York affects Mumbai. It is not because Indian companies suddenly became better or worse. It is because the people controlling that enormous pool of money changed their mind about where they want it to be.

We will go much deeper on these foreign investors — who they actually are, how much money they move, and where to track them — in Part 3 of this series. For now, just hold onto this image: a giant tank of global money, constantly flowing between countries, and India is one of the places it flows into and out of.

Let's Talk About America First

The United States has the world's largest stock market. By a lot. The total value of American stocks is roughly five to six times larger than India's entire market. This means when American investors get nervous and start selling, the ripple effects reach almost every other market in the world — including ours.

When traders in India say "US markets," they usually mean three specific indices. An index, by the way, is just a number that tracks the average performance of a group of stocks. Think of it like a school report card average — instead of every student's individual score, you get one number that tells you how the class did overall.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average (just called "the Dow") This tracks 30 very large, well-known American companies — think Apple, Microsoft, Coca-Cola, Goldman Sachs. It is one of the oldest stock market indices in the world, started in 1896. When people on the news say "the Dow fell 500 points today," they mean the combined average of these 30 companies dropped by that amount. The Dow is not perfect — 30 companies is a small sample — but it is the most widely reported number in financial news, so it shapes sentiment globally.

The S&P 500 This tracks 500 large American companies. It is a much broader and more accurate picture of the US economy than the Dow. Most serious investors watch the S&P 500 more closely than the Dow. When the S&P 500 has a bad day, it usually means a wide range of American industries are struggling — not just 30 specific companies.

The Nasdaq This mostly tracks technology companies — Apple, Google, Meta, Amazon, Netflix, and thousands of smaller tech firms. When the Nasdaq falls sharply, it often means something has spooked technology investors specifically. This matters to India because our IT sector — Infosys, TCS, Wipro, HCL — does a large chunk of its business with American technology companies. If American tech is struggling, Indian IT stocks often feel it too.

When Do US Markets Actually Trade?

US markets open at roughly 7 PM India time and close around 1:30 AM India time. (It shifts slightly between Indian Standard Time and US Daylight Saving adjustments, but this is roughly right for most of the year.)

This means by the time you wake up in the morning, the US market has already finished its entire trading day. Whatever happened there — whether it was a good day or a catastrophic one — is already done. You are reading the result, not watching it live.

This is actually useful. By 7 or 8 AM, you can look at exactly what the US market did and form a view on how it might affect India's opening.

Asian Markets — The Preview Before the Preview

Before India opens at 9:15 AM, a few Asian markets have already been trading for a couple of hours. These give you an additional sense of the morning mood.

Japan — Nikkei 225 Japan's stock market opens around 5:30 AM India time. The Nikkei tracks 225 large Japanese companies. Japan is one of the world's major economies, so a sharp fall in the Nikkei — especially if it is following a bad US night — adds to the weight of negative global sentiment that India might open into.

Hong Kong — Hang Seng Hong Kong's market opens around 6:45 AM India time. The Hang Seng is important because it has significant exposure to large Chinese companies. When China's economy faces trouble or the Chinese government makes a policy announcement that spooks investors, it often shows up here first.

You do not need to follow every Asian market obsessively. What you are looking for is a general mood — are major Asian markets up, down, or flat this morning? If Japan is up 1% and Hong Kong is flat, the morning mood is calm. If both are down sharply following a bad US night, that is a signal that the global selling sentiment is continuing into the Asian session.

Crude Oil — Why India Cares More Than Most Countries

Crude oil is one of those things that affects the Indian market more than most beginners realise.

India imports roughly 85% of its crude oil needs. We don't produce enough domestically, so we buy it from other countries — Saudi Arabia, Iraq, UAE, and others. We pay for it in US Dollars.

When crude oil prices go up significantly, here is what happens inside the Indian economy: the government's import bill gets much larger. Petrol and diesel prices eventually rise. Transport costs go up. This makes everything more expensive — vegetables, manufactured goods, raw materials. Companies that rely heavily on fuel or oil derivatives see their costs rise and their profits fall.

When profits fall across many companies, investors expect lower future earnings. Lower expected earnings means people are less willing to pay high prices for those stocks. So when crude oil spikes sharply, you will often see the Indian market fall — even if nothing specifically Indian went wrong.

The reverse is also true. When crude oil prices fall, India's import costs drop, inflation comes down, company profits improve, and the market often reacts positively.

As of the time of writing, crude oil prices are quoted in US Dollars per barrel. You can check it on any financial site — just search "crude oil price." The number to note is "WTI Crude" (American benchmark) or "Brent Crude" (global benchmark). Either will do.

The Dollar-Rupee Rate — A Quick Note

One more thing that shows up in morning market analysis is the Dollar-Rupee exchange rate — how many rupees it takes to buy one US Dollar.

When the Rupee weakens (meaning it takes more rupees to buy one Dollar), it has mixed effects. It makes Indian exports cheaper for foreign buyers, which is good for export-oriented companies like IT firms. But it makes imports — like crude oil — more expensive in rupee terms, which is bad for companies that import raw materials.

For now, just know that the Dollar-Rupee rate is something traders glance at each morning. A very sharp move in either direction is worth noting. A minor daily fluctuation generally isn't.

Putting It All Together — A Real Example

Let's walk through a real scenario so this isn't just theory.

It is a Wednesday evening. Indian markets just closed at 3:30 PM. You sit down to do your evening market check (we'll build the full checklist for this in Part 5). Everything looks fine in India — Nifty closed up 0.4%, nothing dramatic happened.

At around 9 PM, you check in on the US market, which has been trading for about two hours. You see: - The S&P 500 is down 2.1% - The Nasdaq is down 3.4% - The reason on the news: the US Federal Reserve (America's central bank — roughly equivalent to India's RBI) made a statement suggesting they will keep interest rates higher for longer than expected. This spooked investors.

You go to sleep.

The next morning at 7 AM, you check again: - S&P 500 closed down 2.3% for the night - Japan's Nikkei is down 1.8% following the US - Crude oil is up slightly — not a major move - Rupee is a little weaker against the Dollar — again, not dramatic

What does this tell you about India's 9:15 AM opening?

It is very likely that Nifty will open lower. How much lower? Hard to say exactly — but probably somewhere between 0.8% and 1.5% down, roughly mirroring the global selloff at a slightly muted level. Indian markets tend to absorb global shocks at about half to two-thirds of their intensity, on average.

Now, does this mean you immediately go and sell everything you hold? No. Global selloffs sometimes reverse by mid-day. Sometimes Indian markets "digest" the global bad news by 11 AM and start climbing back. But it does mean: approach tomorrow morning cautiously, maybe wait to see how the first thirty minutes of trading go before making any new buys.

The Honest Part — This Doesn't Always Work the Way You Expect

Here is where we need to be real with you.

Sometimes the US market falls 1% and India opens flat or even green. Sometimes the US has a perfectly good night and India still falls because of something specific to India — a Reserve Bank of India policy announcement, a government regulation change, or a major Indian company announcing bad results.

Global signals are an input, not a guarantee. Think of them as weather forecasts. A forecast of rain doesn't mean it will definitely rain, and it doesn't tell you exactly when or how hard. But it does mean you should probably carry an umbrella.

Experienced traders use global cues to form a morning bias — a starting expectation of which way the market is likely to lean. They don't bet everything on that bias. They hold it loosely and update it as the actual market opens and shows them what it is actually doing.

This will make more sense once you have done this for a few weeks. You'll start to notice which types of global signals India tends to follow closely and which ones it shrugs off. That pattern recognition builds through observation, not through any formula.

Where to Actually Check These Things

You don't need multiple paid subscriptions or complex setups. Here is a simple free setup that covers everything:

For US markets and global indices: TradingView (free tier is enough). Search for "SPX" for the S&P 500, "DJI" for the Dow, "IXIC" for Nasdaq, "NI225" for Nikkei, "HSI" for Hang Seng. Bookmark them. Takes thirty seconds to scan in the morning.

For crude oil: TradingView — search "USOIL" or "UKOIL." Or just Google "crude oil price today."

For Dollar-Rupee: TradingView — search "USDINR." Or your broker's platform usually shows it.

For a quick morning overview: Moneycontrol's market homepage gives a snapshot of global markets, GIFT Nifty (which we will cover in detail in Part 4), and major Indian indices — all on one screen. It is messy and has a lot of ads, but the data is there.

Try This for the Next Five Days

Each morning before 9:15 AM, spend three minutes checking the following and writing it in a notebook:

  1. Did the S&P 500 go up or down last night? By roughly how much?
  2. Are Asian markets (Nikkei, Hang Seng) up or down this morning?
  3. What is crude oil doing — up, down, or roughly flat?

Then note what Nifty actually does when it opens and in the first hour of trading.

After five days, look at your notes. You will start to see patterns. On the days you noted "US down big + Asia down," what did Nifty do? On the days everything was green globally, did India follow?

You are not trying to predict perfectly. You are training your own eyes to see how these connections actually play out in real time — which is far more valuable than reading about it.
