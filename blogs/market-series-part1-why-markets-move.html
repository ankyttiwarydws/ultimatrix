Before You Read Anything Else, Read This

If someone told you to "check the FII data" or "GIFT Nifty is indicating a gap-up," would you know what to do with that information?

Most beginners don't. And that's fine — but it means any article about how to predict tomorrow's market will go completely over your head unless you first understand one very simple thing:

Why does a stock price change at all?

Once you understand this — really understand it, in your gut, not just in theory — everything else starts to make sense. The FII data, the US markets, the crude oil price, all of it. They are all just different ways of answering the same question: are there more buyers or more sellers today?

Let's start from zero.

What Is a Stock Price, Really?

A stock price is not some magical number that a company decides. It is not calculated by a formula. It is not set by the government.

A stock price is simply the last price at which a willing buyer and a willing seller agreed to do a deal.

That's it. Nothing more.

Let's say you have a share of Tata Motors in your Demat account. You decide you want to sell it. You open Zerodha and see the current price is ₹800. You put a sell order for ₹800. Someone on the other side — sitting somewhere in Delhi or Hyderabad, you'll never know who — is willing to pay ₹800 for it. You click sell, they click buy, the deal happens. That moment of agreement? That becomes the new "price" of Tata Motors. Every person looking at the market sees ₹800.

Now imagine one minute later, fifty more people decide they want to buy Tata Motors. But there are only ten people willing to sell. What happens?

The buyers start competing with each other. One person raises their bid to ₹802. Another goes to ₹804. The sellers, seeing the demand, start asking for ₹806, ₹810. Eventually they agree somewhere around ₹808 or ₹810.

The price went up. Not because Tata Motors did anything different. Not because of a news announcement. Just because in that one minute, more people wanted to buy than wanted to sell.

This is the entire stock market. Just millions of these small auctions happening every second.

The Sabzi Mandi Analogy

Think about a vegetable market on a rainy Monday morning. Fewer people showed up to buy — it's raining, most people stayed home. The vendors have the same amount of tomatoes as usual. Too many tomatoes, not enough buyers.

What happens? The vendor at one stall drops his price to ₹30 per kilo to attract the few customers who did come. The vendor next to him also drops to ₹28. By the end of the morning, tomatoes that normally sell for ₹40 are going for ₹25.

Nothing changed about the tomatoes. The quality is the same. The tomatoes didn't become worse overnight. There was simply a day when sellers outnumbered buyers — and the price adjusted.

Now flip it. It's the day before Diwali. Everyone wants tomatoes for cooking. The same vendors have the same supply, but now there are ten times as many buyers crowding around. The price goes to ₹60 per kilo and people still buy.

Same tomatoes. Different balance of buyers and sellers. Completely different price.

The stock market works the same way. Every single day.

So What Makes People Want to Buy or Sell?

Here is where it gets a little more interesting. People don't buy and sell stocks randomly. They are reacting to information — news, results, expectations, rumours, and sometimes just what everyone else around them is doing.

Think about it this way. You hold shares of HDFC Bank. Tonight, HDFC Bank announces that their profits for the quarter are much higher than anyone expected. What happens the next morning? Everyone who was on the fence about buying now wants to buy. The people who were thinking of selling decide to hold and wait for a higher price. More buyers, fewer sellers — the price goes up.

Now the opposite. You hold shares of a pharmaceutical company. Tonight, the news breaks that one of their major drugs has failed a government safety test and been banned. Suddenly, everyone who owns the stock wants to get out. And nobody wants to buy in. More sellers, no buyers — the price crashes.

This is why traders pay so much attention to news, quarterly results, and global events. Not because they are interesting in an academic sense, but because they shift the balance between buyers and sellers.

The Most Important Thing Nobody Explains Clearly

Here is the part that confuses almost every beginner, and once you understand it, you will see the market differently.

The market is not pricing what is happening today. It is pricing what people expect to happen in the future.

Let's use a simple example.

Reliance Industries announces that their profits this quarter are up 20%. Great news, right? You might expect the stock to go up on that announcement.

But what if everyone — all the big analysts, all the institutional investors — had already predicted 25% profit growth, and only 20% came in? To them, the "actual" result is a disappointment compared to the expectation. So they sell. The stock falls even though the profits grew.

This drives beginners crazy. "Why is the stock going down on good news?"

Because the stock had already risen in the weeks before the announcement, pricing in those expectations. The market had already had its reaction to the good news before the news came out. By the time the announcement happened, people who expected 25% are disappointed by 20%, and they sell.

This is why you will often hear traders say: "Buy the rumour, sell the news."

It is not a clever saying. It is a description of how the market actually works. Price moves happen before events, not after them. Because the market is full of people who are all trying to predict what will happen next — and acting on those predictions before the event arrives.

A Quick Recap Before We Go Further

Let's make sure this is crystal clear before you read any further in this series.

Price goes up when: More people want to buy than sell. This happens when people expect good things ahead — strong results, positive news, improving economy, general optimism.

Price goes down when: More people want to sell than buy. This happens when people expect bad things ahead — weak results, bad news, global uncertainty, general fear.

Price stays flat when: The number of buyers and sellers are roughly balanced. Nobody has strong conviction either way.

The stock market on any given day is simply the combined result of millions of people making buy-or-sell decisions based on what they think will happen next. Not what is happening right now. What will happen.

This is why tomorrow's market is affected by what happened in America overnight. By what the oil price is doing. By whether a big foreign fund decided to move money out of India. All of these things change people's expectations about the future — which changes the balance of buyers and sellers — which changes the price.

Why Does Any of This Matter for Tomorrow's Market?

When you sit down in the evening and ask "where will the market go tomorrow?" — what you are really asking is this:

When the market opens at 9:15 AM, will there be more people wanting to buy or more people wanting to sell?

And the answer depends on how people are feeling about the future. Which in turn depends on:

  • What happened in global markets overnight (because Indian investors react to the world)
  • What big investors (FIIs and DIIs) are doing with their money
  • Whether any major news came out after the Indian market closed
  • What the chart pattern says about which direction has more momentum
  • Whether any big economic event is coming up that might shake things up

Each of these is a separate article in this series. But all of them are just trying to answer the same basic question: tomorrow morning, will there be more hands reaching out to buy, or more hands reaching out to sell?

Now that you understand the foundation, let's build on it.

One Thing to Try This Week

Before you read the next article, try this. Pick any one stock you know — Infosys, Reliance, HDFC Bank, whatever you've heard of. Open any free charting website (TradingView is fine, it's free). Look at the daily chart for the last three months.

Find one day where the price dropped sharply. Then try to find out what news came out that day. Google the company name and the date. Nine times out of ten, you'll find a clear reason — bad results, negative news, something that made sellers outnumber buyers that day.

Then find a day where the price jumped. Do the same thing.

You'll start to see that price movements are almost never random. They are reactions to information — real or expected. Once you start seeing that connection, you'll begin to understand what to look for before the market opens.
