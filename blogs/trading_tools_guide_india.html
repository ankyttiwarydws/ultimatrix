There Are a Lot of Tools Out There. Which Ones Actually Matter?

If you've spent any time in trading communities — Telegram groups, YouTube comments, Twitter/X — you've heard names like Screener.in, ChartInk, ScanX, TradingView thrown around constantly.

"I found this on Screener." "ChartInk scan gave me 12 stocks today." "Check TradingView for the setup."

And if you're a beginner, you're probably thinking: What are all these? Do I need all of them? Where do I even start?

This article answers exactly that. No hype. No affiliate recommendations. Just a plain explanation of what each tool is, what problem it solves, and how traders actually use it.

First, Understand That Different Tools Solve Different Problems

This is the most important thing to understand before we get into the list.

These tools are not competitors doing the same thing. They're more like different instruments in a band — each has its own job.

Here's a simple way to think about it:

| What You're Trying to Do | Type of Tool You Need | |---|---| | Research a company's financials | Fundamental analysis tool | | Find stocks that match certain criteria | Stock screener | | Read charts and identify patterns | Charting platform | | Get alerts when stocks cross a level | Scanner / alert tool | | Understand options data | Options analytics tool | | Track news and announcements | News aggregator |

Once you understand which category a tool belongs to, it's much easier to understand what it's for — and whether you need it.

The Tools, Explained Simply

1. Screener.in — For Understanding a Company's Financial Health

What it is: A website where you can look at the detailed financial history of any listed Indian company — going back 10 years in some cases.

Who it's mainly for: People interested in investing or swing trading based on fundamentals. Also useful for any trader who wants to quickly understand whether a company is financially strong or weak before trading its stock.

What you can find on it:

When you type in any company name on Screener.in, you get a clean breakdown of: - Revenue and profit over the last several years (is this company actually growing?) - Debt levels (is it drowning in loans?) - Return on equity (is it using investor money efficiently?) - Promoter holding (are the founders/owners increasing or reducing their stake?) - Peer comparison (how does it compare to competitors in the same industry?)

A practical example of how traders use it:

Say you're looking at a stock that has been moving sharply upward for three days. Before trading it, a quick Screener check might tell you: profits are consistently growing, promoter holding is stable, debt is manageable. That's context — it doesn't tell you where to enter, but it tells you you're looking at a fundamentally sound business rather than a shaky one.

Alternatively, if you see a stock with falling profits and increasing debt suddenly running up 15%, that's a different kind of trade with different risk.

Free or paid? Screener has a very useful free version. The paid version unlocks more detailed data and custom formula screeners.

Best for: Long-term investors, positional traders, anyone doing fundamental due diligence.

2. TradingView — The Charting Platform Most Traders Use

What it is: A web-based charting platform where you can look at price charts for stocks, indices, commodities, currencies — pretty much anything.

Who it's for: Anyone who reads charts. Intraday traders, swing traders, investors — TradingView is genuinely useful across the board.

Why traders love it:

TradingView has clean, fast, customisable charts. You can add dozens of indicators (VWAP, RSI, Bollinger Bands, EMA — whatever you use), draw trendlines, mark support and resistance levels, and save your chart layouts.

It also has a scripting language called Pine Script that lets you create custom indicators or test trading ideas. You don't need to be a programmer to use existing scripts — thousands of free community scripts are available.

Beyond charts, TradingView has: - A built-in screener (basic, but useful) - A news feed - A social community where traders share chart ideas - Multi-timeframe viewing (see 5-min, 15-min, and daily charts side by side)

The free version reality:

The free version is genuinely good for beginners. You get a limited number of indicators per chart and limited saved layouts — but for basic chart reading and learning, it's more than enough. Paid plans start unlocking more indicators per chart, more alerts, and more advanced features.

One thing to know: TradingView is not a broker. You can't place trades directly from TradingView (unless you connect it to a supporting broker). It's purely for analysis.

Best for: Anyone who works with charts. It's the most widely used charting tool among Indian traders and worth learning regardless of your trading style.

3. ChartInk — For Finding Stocks That Match a Pattern Right Now

What it is: A stock scanner that lets you find stocks matching specific technical criteria — in real time during market hours or after close.

The simplest way to explain it:

Imagine you believe that stocks crossing above their 20-day moving average tend to have good momentum. But there are 5,000+ stocks listed in India. You can't manually check all of them.

ChartInk does it for you. You create (or use an existing) scan: "Show me all NSE-listed stocks where today's closing price crossed above the 20-day moving average." ChartInk runs this across the entire market in seconds and gives you a list.

What kind of scans are possible:

  • Stocks making 52-week highs
  • Stocks with RSI crossing above 60
  • Stocks with high volume breakouts
  • Stocks where MACD just turned bullish
  • Any combination of technical conditions you define

Pre-built scans vs custom scans:

ChartInk has hundreds of pre-built scans shared by the community. Beginners can start using these immediately without knowing how to write scan conditions. As you learn more, you can build your own.

Free or paid? ChartInk has a free version with useful scans. The paid version allows more saved scans, intraday scanning, and real-time alerts.

Best for: Traders looking to narrow down a large universe of stocks to a shortlist based on technical criteria. Very popular among swing traders and positional traders in India.

4. ScanX — Real-Time Scanning with Alerts

What it is: Similar in concept to ChartInk, but ScanX is built specifically for speed and real-time intraday scanning.

How it differs from ChartInk:

ChartInk is excellent for end-of-day and positional scans. ScanX is focused more on intraday — it scans the market in near real-time as stocks move during market hours and alerts you when conditions are met.

For example: "Alert me when any Nifty 500 stock breaks out of a 15-minute consolidation with volume above its 10-day average." ScanX watches this live throughout the day and notifies you.

Why this matters for intraday traders:

One of the biggest challenges in intraday trading is finding the right stock at the right moment. You can't watch 500 charts at the same time. Real-time scanners like ScanX become the trader's eyes — doing the watching so you can focus on analysis and execution.

Free or paid? ScanX has both free and paid tiers. The more powerful real-time features and customisation typically require a paid plan.

Best for: Active intraday and momentum traders who need to scan the market live during trading hours.

5. Sensibull — For Options Traders

What it is: An options analytics platform built specifically for the Indian market. It's integrated with most major Indian brokers.

Why options traders need a separate tool:

Options are more complex than stocks. Each option has strike price, expiry, IV, delta, theta, and a lot more moving parts. Trying to manage an options trade from a basic broker interface can be genuinely confusing.

Sensibull simplifies this. It shows you: - Options chain with IV and Greeks (delta, theta, etc.) clearly laid out - Strategy builder where you can visually construct multi-leg strategies (like straddles, strangles, spreads) - Payoff charts showing exactly how much you make or lose at different price levels - Max Pain and PCR data - Options buying and selling directly integrated with your broker

A practical example:

Say you want to sell a Nifty straddle before a big event. Instead of manually calculating your max profit, breakeven points, and maximum loss — Sensibull's strategy builder shows you all of this visually before you place the trade. You can even adjust strikes and expiries interactively and see the payoff graph update in real time.

Free or paid? Sensibull has a free version with basic features. The more advanced strategy analysis and portfolio features are paid.

Best for: Anyone trading options in India. Even basic familiarity with Sensibull will save you from making the kind of mistakes that come from not fully understanding your options position.

6. Opstra — Advanced Options Analytics

What it is: Similar to Sensibull, but with a focus on more advanced options analysis. Opstra is particularly popular among serious options traders for its IV analysis, strategy comparison tools, and backtesting features.

What makes it different from Sensibull:

Both tools overlap significantly. Opstra tends to have more detailed IV analytics — you can track IV percentile (is current IV high or low relative to its history?), IV rank, and historical IV charts for individual stocks. This is very useful for deciding whether to buy or sell options premium.

It also has a strong options strategy comparison feature and backtesting capability, which lets you test how a strategy would have performed historically.

Free or paid? Opstra has a free version and paid tiers.

Best for: Options traders who want deeper volatility analytics and strategy backtesting.

7. NSE India Website (nseindia.com) — Free Official Data

What it is: The official website of the National Stock Exchange of India.

This one gets overlooked by beginners who immediately jump to third-party tools. But the NSE website has some genuinely valuable free data:

  • Option chain with live OI, volume, IV data for all Nifty and Banknifty contracts
  • India VIX live chart
  • PCR data (daily historical)
  • FII/DII activity — how much foreign and domestic institutional investors bought or sold each day
  • Bulk and block deals — when large institutions transact, these get reported
  • Corporate announcements, results calendar

Why the FII/DII data matters:

This is one of the most underused free resources. FII (Foreign Institutional Investors) and DII (Domestic Institutional Investors) are the big players. When FIIs are consistently net buyers (buying more than selling), it often supports the market. When they turn consistently net sellers, markets tend to struggle.

This doesn't predict tomorrow's move. But over weeks and months, tracking this flow gives you a sense of whether big money is coming into or leaving Indian markets.

Best for: Everyone. Before paying for any tool, learn what's available for free here.

8. Moneycontrol / Economic Times Markets — News and Fundamentals

What they are: Financial news portals with market data, company news, and analyst reports.

These are less "tools" and more reference points — but they're part of every trader's daily routine.

What traders use them for: - Checking results calendar (which companies are announcing quarterly results soon) - Tracking corporate news that might affect stock prices - Reading sector-level developments - Checking basic financial ratios quickly

One practical tip: Before trading any stock, a quick Moneycontrol check for recent news can prevent costly surprises. Sometimes a stock is moving because of results, management changes, or sector news — knowing this changes how you think about the trade.

How These Tools Typically Work Together

No trader uses one tool in isolation. Here's how a typical research flow might look for different types of traders:

For a swing trader looking for momentum stocks: 1. Run a ChartInk scan after market close — stocks making new 52-week highs with increasing volume 2. Open the shortlisted stocks on TradingView to study the chart structure 3. Quick Screener.in check on the fundamentals of promising ones 4. Check NSE for any upcoming results or announcements that could affect the trade

For an intraday trader: 1. Check India VIX on NSE before the day starts — high VIX = more volatile day, adjust position sizes 2. Review FII/DII data from previous day for overall market bias 3. Use ScanX during the day to flag real-time momentum opportunities 4. Execute and analyse charts on TradingView or broker terminal

For an options trader: 1. Check PCR and Max Pain on Sensibull or NSE option chain 2. Review IV levels on Opstra — is IV elevated or depressed for this expiry? 3. Build and analyse the strategy on Sensibull's payoff diagram before placing

A Suggested Learning Path for Beginners

You don't need to learn all of these tools at once. Here's a sensible order:

Start here (free, high value): - NSE website — understand the option chain and FII data - TradingView free version — learn to read charts - Screener.in free version — understand how to research companies

Add these once you're comfortable: - ChartInk — start with pre-built scans to find stock ideas - Sensibull free version — if you're getting into options

Later, if you're active: - ScanX — when you start intraday trading seriously - Opstra — when you want deeper options analytics - TradingView paid — when you need more indicators and alerts

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Do I need to pay for all these tools to trade seriously? Absolutely not. TradingView free, Screener.in free, NSE website, and ChartInk free collectively give you a very solid foundation. Most successful traders started with free tools and only paid for upgrades when they had clear specific needs.

Q: Which single tool should a complete beginner start with? TradingView. It's where you'll spend the most time as a chart-based trader, the free version is genuinely useful, and the learning curve is manageable.

Q: Are there tools specific to BSE or only NSE? Most tools support both NSE and BSE data. For options specifically, the active markets are primarily NSE (Nifty, BankNifty, midcap index options, and major stock options). BSE has its own derivatives market but NSE dominates options trading volume in India.

Q: Can I use TradingView to place trades with my Zerodha or Groww account? TradingView has broker integrations, but the specific brokers supported can change. Check TradingView's official broker integration page and your broker's platform for current options. Zerodha's Kite has its own chart interface that many traders use for execution.

Q: Is there a tool that does everything in one place? Not really — and that's actually fine. Different specialised tools tend to do their specific job better than a single all-in-one platform. Most experienced traders end up with two or three tools they use regularly, each for a different purpose.
