You Know the Rules. So Why Does the Account Keep Going Sideways?

You are not a beginner anymore. You know what a stop loss is. You understand the 2% rule. You have read about position sizing, trading psychology, keeping a journal. You have probably watched more trading content than you care to admit.

And yet.

The account is not growing the way it should. Maybe you have a good month and then a bad one that takes most of it back. Maybe you follow your rules perfectly for three weeks and then have one session where everything falls apart and you give back two weeks of gains in a single afternoon. Maybe you look at your trade history and see a pattern you cannot quite name — a recurring situation where something goes wrong that you did not intend.

This is the intermediate plateau. And it is where most traders quietly give up — not because they are incapable, but because they are solving the wrong problem.

The beginner's problem is not knowing the rules. That is fixed with information.

The intermediate trader's problem is not following the rules under pressure. That is a completely different problem — and information alone does not fix it.

This article is about diagnosing exactly where your specific discipline breaks down, and building a personalised structure that addresses it directly.

Why Plan Adherence Fails Under Pressure

Let's start with the honest explanation of why intelligent people with good trading plans keep breaking them.

When you are calm — at your desk in the evening, planning tomorrow's trades, writing in your journal — your thinking is clear and rational. You can see the plan, the risk, the logic. You decide: if this stock falls to ₹185, I exit. Simple.

The next morning, you enter the trade. The stock falls to ₹190. Then ₹187. Then ₹185.

And something happens that no trading book quite prepares you for. The rational plan you made last night suddenly has competition — from a voice in your head that is generating very convincing counter-arguments in real time.

"It's just hitting the stop loss to shake out weak hands before moving higher." "The overall market is down today, it'll recover when sentiment improves." "I've done my research on this company. The fundamentals haven't changed." "If I exit now, I lock in a real loss. If I hold, it might come back."

These thoughts feel like analysis. They are not analysis. They are rationalisation — your brain working backwards from the conclusion it wants (avoid the pain of realising a loss) and constructing reasons to support it.

This is not a character flaw. It is human neuroscience. The part of your brain that processes financial loss activates the same regions as physical pain. Your brain is trying to help you avoid pain. It just does so in a way that makes you a worse trader.

Understanding this is the first step. But understanding it does not make it stop happening. What stops it from damaging your account is structure — rules that are specific enough and automatic enough that the rationalising voice does not get a vote.

The 20-Trade Audit — Find Your Specific Pattern

Here is the most useful exercise an intermediate trader can do. It will take about an hour. It will show you more about your trading than six months of reading theory.

Go back through your last 20 completed trades. For each one, gather this information — from your broker's trade history if you do not have a journal:

  • Entry price and date
  • Exit price and date
  • Was a stop loss planned before entry? Yes / No
  • Was the planned stop loss followed? Yes / No / Modified
  • Reason for exit: stop loss hit / target hit / manual decision
  • Result: profit or loss, in rupees and percentage

Now look at the full picture and answer these questions honestly.

Question one: What percentage of your trades had a planned stop loss before entry?

If the answer is less than 80%, the primary problem is pre-trade planning. You are entering trades without fully deciding what "wrong" looks like. Fix this first, before anything else.

Question two: Of the trades where you had a stop loss, what percentage did you actually follow?

This number is almost always lower than traders expect when they first do this audit. If you planned a stop loss but modified or ignored it more than 20% of the time, stop-loss adherence is your specific weak point. The problem is not knowing the rule — it is following it at the exact moment it matters most.

Question three: Look at your losing trades specifically. How do they end?

There are two common patterns. The first: losses are small and fairly uniform — the stop loss worked, you exited at the planned level, the loss was within the 2% rule. This is healthy. The second: most small losses are fine, but there are one or two large losses that are significantly bigger than everything else. These outlier losses are almost always the result of a stop loss not being followed.

If one pattern emerges — a few large losses dragging down an otherwise reasonable record — the question becomes: what was different about those specific trades? What made you hold those longer than the plan said to?

Question four: Look at your winning trades. How do they end?

Another common pattern: winners are closed too early — sometimes before even reaching a reasonable target — while losers are held longer than they should be. This produces a situation where your average win is smaller than your average loss, which means you need to be right more than 50% of the time just to break even. Most traders cannot sustain that kind of accuracy.

If this is your pattern, the problem is not your entries. It is an asymmetry in how you manage winners versus losers — cutting the winners short because you are afraid of giving back the profit, and holding the losers long because you are afraid of realising the loss.

Write down what you find. The pattern in your last 20 trades is almost certainly present in your next 20 as well — unless you address it specifically.

The Four Most Common Intermediate Weak Points

Based on the audit above, most intermediate traders fall into one of four specific patterns. Read through these and see which one fits.

Weak point one: The Stop-Loss Mover

This trader sets a stop loss before every trade — good discipline — but when the price approaches the stop, they move it lower. Just a little. Just this once. To give the trade more room.

Moving a stop loss in the direction of the trade is almost always a mistake. The stop loss was placed where it was for a reason — because that price level represented the point at which the trade thesis was wrong. Moving it means staying in a trade that is telling you it is not working.

The fix: treat your stop loss as completely immovable once a trade is open. If you want to give a trade more room, plan for that before entry — set a wider stop loss from the start, and size the position smaller to keep the total risk within 2%. Never move a stop loss after the trade is open.

Weak point two: The Revenge Trader

This trader has a losing trade — a frustrating one, maybe one where they did everything right and the market just moved against them. They feel the sting of the loss and almost immediately look for another trade to make it back.

The revenge trade is almost always placed without proper analysis, at a size larger than usual, in a volatile market condition, while the trader's emotional state is compromised. It loses more often than not. And then there is the urge to place a third trade to make back both losses.

This is one of the fastest ways to turn a manageable bad day into a catastrophic one.

The fix: a mandatory cooling-off rule after a losing trade. At a minimum, no new trade for thirty minutes after a stop loss is hit. Ideally, for intermediate traders still building discipline, the rule is: after two consecutive losses in a day, trading is done for that day. Full stop. Close the platform. The market will be there tomorrow.

Weak point three: The Early Winner

This trader exits profitable trades far too soon — sometimes locking in a 0.5% gain when the trade was planned for a 2% target. The reasoning is understandable: a profit is real, a profit in hand is safe, and the market might reverse. Better to take the small gain than risk giving it back.

The problem is mathematical. If your average win is ₹500 and your average loss — because you follow your stop loss — is ₹1,000, you need to be right twice for every one time you are wrong just to break even. Over many trades, this arithmetic is impossible to sustain.

The fix: set a target before entry — just like the stop loss — and let the trade run to it. If you want to partially exit early (close half the position at a smaller gain and let the other half run), that is a structured approach and acceptable. What is not acceptable is closing the entire trade at 30% of the planned target because you felt nervous.

Weak point four: The Overtrader

This trader does not have a problem with any single trade. Their stop losses are followed, their winners are held to target, their position sizes are correct. But they trade too frequently. Twenty trades in a week when five would have been better. Entering positions in unclear market conditions because being in a trade feels more active than sitting in cash.

Each extra trade adds transaction costs — brokerage, STT, exchange charges — that accumulate quietly. Each extra trade in unclear conditions has a lower probability of success. And the cognitive load of managing many positions simultaneously degrades decision quality on all of them.

The fix: a maximum trades per week rule. For most intermediate traders, five to eight trades per week in equities is plenty. If you find yourself looking for a trade because you are bored or because you feel like you should be doing something, that is the signal to close the platform and do something else.

Building Your Personalised Rulebook

Generic trading rules are a starting point. What actually changes behaviour is a rulebook built around your specific weak point — the one the audit revealed.

Here is how to build one.

Start with the universal rules that everyone needs:

  • No trade without a pre-defined stop loss.
  • Maximum risk per trade: 2% of total capital.
  • Equity delivery only if you are still building consistency. (Add intraday only after three consecutive months of following rules.)
  • Monthly review: 30 minutes, every month, without skipping.

Then add the rules specific to your weak point:

If you are a Stop-Loss Mover: "Once a trade is open, the stop loss price cannot be changed. If I want to exit before the stop loss, I can. If I want to stay in after the stop loss is hit, I cannot. The stop loss is the final word."

If you are a Revenge Trader: "After any losing trade, I will wait a minimum of 45 minutes before placing the next trade. After two consecutive losing trades in a single day, I will close the platform and not trade again until the following morning."

If you are an Early Winner: "I will set a target price before every trade. I will not close more than 50% of a position before the target is reached. If I want to exit entirely before the target, I must write down the reason in my journal before executing the exit."

If you are an Overtrader: "I will place a maximum of six trades per week. Before placing any trade, I will ask: is this a genuinely good setup, or am I trading because I feel like I should be doing something? If the answer is the second, I will not place the trade."

Write these rules down. Keep them somewhere visible — a printed page next to your trading setup, a note on your phone, the first page of your trading journal. Read them before every trading session.

The point of writing them down is not ceremonial. It is practical: rules that exist only in your head are much easier for the rationalising voice to override than rules that are written, specific, and in front of you.

The Journal That Actually Helps

Many traders keep a trading journal that records what they bought and sold and whether they made or lost money. This is better than nothing, but it misses the most important part.

A journal that actually improves your trading records the emotional state and decision-making process around each trade — not just the outcome.

For each trade, write:

Before the trade: - Why am I entering this trade? (One or two sentences of actual reasoning, not "it looks good") - Where is my stop loss, and why at that specific price? - Where is my target, and why? - What is my maximum loss on this trade in rupees? - How am I feeling right now? (Calm / slightly anxious / excited / frustrated)

After the trade: - Did I follow my plan? - If I deviated, what was the thought that led to the deviation? - What did this trade teach me?

The emotional state question before the trade is the most valuable one. Over time, you will notice a pattern: the trades placed when you are frustrated, excited, or in a hurry to recover a loss perform significantly worse than trades placed when you are calm and have no particular emotional stake in the outcome.

This is data. Use it. If you find that your worst trades consistently happen on afternoons after a morning loss, add a rule: no trading after 1 PM on a day where you had a stop loss triggered before noon.

Personalised rules built from your own data are far more powerful than generic advice — because they address your specific patterns, in your specific context, with evidence from your own trading history.

The One Metric That Tells You If You Are Improving

There are many numbers you can track. But if you want one single metric that honestly measures whether your risk management is improving, it is this:

Your average loss divided by your average win — called the loss-to-win ratio.

Calculate it from your last 20 trades. Add up all your losses and divide by the number of losing trades to get the average loss. Add up all your wins and divide by the number of winning trades to get the average win.

If your average loss is ₹1,200 and your average win is ₹800, your ratio is 1.5 — meaning you are losing 1.5 times what you are winning. With this ratio, you need to be right more than 60% of the time to break even. That is very difficult to sustain.

If your average loss is ₹800 and your average win is ₹1,200, your ratio is 0.67 — meaning you are winning 1.5 times what you are losing. With this ratio, you only need to be right 40% of the time to break even. Being right 50% of the time makes you consistently profitable. That is a sustainable business.

The goal for an intermediate trader is to get this ratio below 1.0 — average wins larger than average losses. This is achieved not by picking better stocks, but by following stop losses (limiting average loss) and letting winners run to target (increasing average win).

Track this number monthly. It is the most honest measure of whether the discipline is actually improving.

What the Next Six Months Should Look Like

If you are an intermediate trader who has done the audit, identified your weak point, and built a personalised rulebook, here is what the next six months of focused practice should produce:

Your plan-adherence rate — the percentage of trades where you follow your rules — should climb from wherever it is now toward 85–90%. It will not be perfect. But it should be measurably improving month by month.

Your loss-to-win ratio should move below 1.0 — even if your win rate stays the same or even drops slightly. Smaller losses and larger wins is the goal, not a higher percentage of winning trades.

Your worst month should not be dramatically worse than your average month. If your average month is flat to slightly positive, a bad month should be mildly negative — not a catastrophic loss that requires months of recovery. The rules should contain the downside even when your judgment is off.

By the end of six months of this kind of focused, audit-driven practice, you will have something more valuable than a profitable month. You will have a genuine understanding of how you specifically trade — your strengths, your weak points, your emotional triggers. That self-knowledge is what separates traders who scale successfully from traders who stay stuck.

Which brings us to C3.

Try This This Week

Do the 20-trade audit. Block one hour, pull up your trade history from the last three to six months, and work through the four questions in the audit section above.

Write down your weak point — honestly, without softening it. Then write one specific rule that directly addresses that weak point. Just one rule, specific enough that there is no ambiguity about whether you are following it or not.

Trade with that rule for the next four weeks. Review at the end of the month. Did it help? Did a different weak point emerge once this one was addressed?

This is how trading discipline is actually built — one specific, tested, refined rule at a time.
