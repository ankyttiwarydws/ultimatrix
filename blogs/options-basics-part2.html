Welcome Back — This Is the Part That Actually Saves You Money

In Part 1, we covered what options are. You understand calls, puts, premiums, strike prices, and expiry. The basic idea is clear.

Part 2 is where most beginner options content stops being honest.

Because knowing what an option is and knowing what an option does to your money in practice are two very different things. The gap between those two pieces of knowledge is where most beginners lose money — not through bad luck, but through not being warned about things that are completely predictable and completely knowable in advance.

This part covers three things that every options buyer needs to understand before placing a single trade. Then we will look at what options are genuinely useful for — because they are not just speculation tools, and understanding their real purpose makes you a better user of them.

Let's go.

The Thing Nobody Explains Properly: Your Premium Is Melting

When you buy a call or put option, you pay a premium. In Part 1, we treated the premium as a fixed amount — you pay ₹150, that is your maximum loss, done.

The reality is more uncomfortable than that.

The premium is not sitting still. From the moment you buy the option, the premium is slowly losing value every single day — even if the underlying asset (Nifty, or the stock) does not move at all. Even on days when the market is completely flat, your option is worth less than it was yesterday.

This is called time decay. And it is the single most important concept in options trading that beginners consistently underestimate.

Here is the intuition behind it.

An option's premium has two components:

Intrinsic value — the real, mathematical value of the option right now. If you have a call option with a strike of 22,000 and Nifty is currently at 22,300, the intrinsic value is 300 points. That is real, tangible value.

Time value — the extra amount you are paying for the possibility that things could get even better before expiry. If the same call option's premium is ₹380, but the intrinsic value is only ₹300, the remaining ₹80 is time value. You are paying ₹80 for the chance that Nifty might go even higher before the option expires.

Here is what makes time value dangerous for buyers: it disappears as time passes, regardless of what the market does. Every day that goes by, the option has less time remaining for the hoped-for move to happen. Less time means less possibility. Less possibility means less time value. Less time value means a lower premium.

The pace of decay accelerates as expiry approaches.

This is the part that really matters. Time decay is not linear — it does not take away the same amount each day. It is slow in the early part of an option's life and then gets faster and faster as expiry approaches.

Imagine an ice cube. In a cool room, it melts slowly for the first hour. But as it gets smaller, it melts faster and faster, until in the final minutes it is disappearing rapidly. Options time value behaves almost exactly like this.

In practical terms: an option with 30 days left loses time value slowly. The same option with 5 days left is losing value much faster every single day. An option on its expiry day — Thursday in Indian markets — can lose most of its remaining value in a matter of hours if the market is not moving in its favour.

What this means for you as a buyer:

Every day you hold an option, you are fighting against time decay. The market needs to move in your direction not just eventually, but quickly enough to outpace the decay eating away at your premium.

A flat market is not neutral for an options buyer. A flat market is a slow, steady loss. The calendar is always working against you.

The Cheap Premium Trap — Why ₹20 Options Are Almost Never a Good Idea

Here is the most common and most expensive mistake beginner options traders make in India.

Nifty is at 22,000. A trader thinks Nifty will go up. They look at the option chain — the list of available options at different strike prices — and see something interesting.

A call option at the 22,000 strike (at the money) costs ₹150 per unit. For one lot of 75 units, that is ₹11,250.

But a call option at the 22,500 strike (out of the money) costs only ₹30 per unit. For one lot, that is just ₹2,250.

And a call option at the 23,000 strike (far out of the money) costs ₹8 per unit. For one lot, that is ₹600.

The beginner looks at these numbers and thinks: "Why would I pay ₹11,250 when I can get the same bet for ₹600? If Nifty goes up, I make money either way. And if it doesn't, I only lose ₹600 instead of ₹11,250."

This logic feels completely reasonable. It is almost completely wrong.

Here is why.

The 22,000 strike call (ATM): Nifty needs to go above 22,150 (the strike plus the premium paid) for this to be profitable at expiry. A move of 150 points — less than 1% — puts you in profit territory.

The 22,500 strike call (OTM): Nifty needs to go above 22,530 for this to be profitable at expiry. That is a move of 530 points — more than 2.4% — just to break even. For a meaningful profit, you need 600, 700 points or more.

The 23,000 strike call (far OTM): Nifty needs to go above 23,008 for this to be profitable. That is a move of over 1,000 points — more than 4.5% — in the time remaining until expiry. Nifty moving 4.5% in a month is not impossible, but it is genuinely uncommon.

The ₹600 option is cheap because the probability of it making money is very, very low. Options are priced by the market with sophisticated models. The lower the probability of an option finishing in the money, the lower the premium. You are not getting a bargain — you are paying a small amount for a very unlikely outcome.

The lottery ticket comparison:

A lottery ticket costs ₹10 and has a small chance of winning ₹1 crore. The expected value — what you get on average — is less than ₹10, because the probability of winning is tiny. Most tickets are worthless.

Far out-of-the-money options work similarly. They are cheap because most of the time, they expire worthless. The few times they pay off, they pay off dramatically — which is what gets shown in the WhatsApp trading groups and YouTube videos. The many more times they expire at zero do not get shared.

What should you do instead?

If you are buying options, buying at-the-money or slightly in-the-money options gives you a much higher probability of the option having some value at expiry. Yes, the premium is higher. But the option does not need the market to make a heroic move for it to work. A moderate, reasonable move in your direction is enough.

Cheap out-of-the-money options are very rarely good value. The lower price reflects the lower probability, not a market mistake.

Being Right and Still Losing — The Timing Problem

This is the one that frustrates people the most — because it feels deeply unfair when it happens.

You analysed the market. You were right about direction. Nifty went up. And you still lost money on your call option.

How?

Because in options, being right about direction is only one of three things you need to be right about. You also need to be right about magnitude (how much it moves) and timing (when it moves).

Let's walk through a specific example so this is completely clear.

It is the beginning of the month. Nifty is at 22,000. You are confident it will rise. You buy a call option: - Strike price: 22,000 - Expiry: Last Thursday of the month — 30 days away - Premium paid: ₹200 per unit - Total cost: ₹200 × 75 = ₹15,000

Now let's say Nifty moves, but slowly. By day 20, Nifty is at 22,100. You were right — it went up. But here is what has happened to your option:

The intrinsic value is now ₹100 (Nifty at 22,100 minus strike at 22,000). But the time value has collapsed — there are only 10 days left until expiry. The total premium is now perhaps ₹110.

You paid ₹200. It is now worth ₹110. You have lost ₹90 per unit — ₹6,750 total — even though Nifty moved in your direction.

For you to break even, Nifty needs to be above 22,200 (strike plus premium paid) at expiry. It is currently at 22,100. You need another 100 points in 10 days.

Maybe it gets there. Maybe it does not. But the point is: on day 20, with Nifty having moved up 100 points, you are still sitting on a loss. The time decay ate the profit that the price move should have generated.

The three things options buyers need to get right:

One — Direction. The price must move the way you expect. (Call = up, Put = down.)

Two — Magnitude. It must move enough to cover the premium you paid and then some.

Three — Timing. It must move enough, in the right direction, before the option expires.

Get all three right: you profit, sometimes dramatically. Get the direction right but not the magnitude or timing: you still lose money. Get the direction wrong: you lose the full premium.

This is why experienced traders say that buying options is harder than it looks. You are not just predicting a direction — you are predicting a sufficient move in that direction within a specific time window. That is a much harder task.

The Other Side: Option Sellers

So far, we have only talked about buying options — paying the premium and getting the right.

There is another side to every options trade: the seller. For every call you buy, someone sells it to you. For every put you buy, someone sells it to you.

What does the seller get? They receive your premium upfront. They keep it if the option expires worthless.

What does the seller risk? If the option moves against them — if you are right — they can face losses that are much larger than the premium they collected. In fact, for call sellers, losses can theoretically be unlimited if the underlying keeps rising.

This might make selling options sound reckless. But here is an interesting fact: time decay works in the seller's favour. Every day that passes, the premium the seller collected is a little more secure — because the option is a little more likely to expire worthless. Sellers are on the right side of time.

This is why many experienced traders are net sellers of options rather than buyers. They collect premiums and benefit from time decay rather than fighting against it.

But — and this is important — selling options requires significantly more capital, more sophistication, and a very specific understanding of risk management. The losses when wrong can be very large and very fast. Option selling is not a beginner's approach.

We mention it here because understanding that sellers exist — and that they are often experienced, well-capitalised traders on the other side of the beginner's trade — is important context. When you buy a cheap out-of-the-money option, the person selling it to you has done the probability maths. They believe, statistically, they will keep your premium most of the time.

What Options Are Actually Useful For

After all of this — time decay, the cheap premium trap, the timing problem — you might be wondering: is there a legitimate reason to use options at all?

Absolutely. Options were not invented as a way to lose money quickly. They were invented for very specific, genuinely useful purposes. Here are the main ones.

Use one — Hedging an existing portfolio

This is the original purpose of put options, and it is the most rational use.

Imagine you own ₹5 lakh worth of Nifty stocks and you are nervous about a short-term fall — perhaps a major event is coming up, or global markets look shaky. You do not want to sell your stocks because you believe in them long-term. But you want protection for the next month.

You buy a Nifty put option. If Nifty falls, your put option gains value — partially offsetting the losses on your stock portfolio. If Nifty rises, your put expires worthless, but your portfolio is now worth more than before. The premium you paid is essentially the cost of insurance.

This is exactly what the flat owner was doing in our Part 1 analogy. It is rational, conservative, and a genuinely good use of options.

Use two — Defined risk directional trades

Sometimes you have a strong view on a stock or index and you want to express that view. Options allow you to do this with a defined, known maximum loss — the premium you pay.

Compare this to buying the stock directly: if you put ₹1 lakh into a stock and it falls 30%, you lose ₹30,000. If instead you buy a call option for ₹8,000, your maximum loss is ₹8,000 — no matter what happens. The stock can go to zero and you still only lose the premium.

For speculative, high-conviction trades where you want to limit the downside to a specific amount, options can be the right tool.

Use three — Generating income from stocks you already own

Experienced traders who own stocks can sell call options against their holdings — called covered calls — to collect premium income. The strategy has trade-offs and nuances, but it is a legitimate income-generating approach for long-term investors with substantial portfolios.

Are You Ready for Options? An Honest Self-Check

Before we close, here is a set of honest questions. Answer them truthfully — not the way you wish they were, but the way they actually are.

Have you been trading equities — actual stocks, in delivery — for at least one year?

If not: options are not the right next step. The discipline of following a plan, managing losses, and understanding how markets move needs to be built in a simpler environment first. Go back to equity delivery trading and build that foundation.

Do you understand clearly why the premium on an option you are looking at is the price it is — not just what it costs, but why the market has priced it there?

If you cannot answer this: the cheap premium trap is waiting for you. Take more time to understand option pricing before trading.

Can you afford to lose the entire premium on every options trade you place — without it affecting your life, your mood, or your next month's budget?

If no: your position sizes are too large. Options premiums going to zero is not a rare outcome. It is a completely normal and frequent outcome. Every rupee you put into an options premium should be money you have mentally already accepted as gone.

Do you have a specific reason — hedging, a high-conviction trade with defined risk — for using options rather than just buying the stock directly?

If the answer is "options seem exciting" or "I can make more money faster": that is not a reason. That is how the premium trap catches you. Options are tools for specific situations, not a shortcut to bigger returns.

If you answered yes honestly to all four: you have a reasonable foundation to begin exploring options carefully, with small position sizes, and with the expectation that the first few months are about learning — not profits.

If you answered no to any of them: that is not a failure. It is useful information. It tells you exactly what to work on before adding options to your toolkit.

The Summary Across Both Parts

Let's close with the complete picture in plain language.

An option is the right — not the obligation — to buy or sell something at a fixed price before a fixed date. You pay a premium for this right. The premium is your maximum loss as a buyer.

A call option profits when the price rises significantly above the strike before expiry.

A put option profits when the price falls significantly below the strike before expiry.

Time decay works against buyers every single day — your premium loses value as time passes, even when the market is flat.

Cheap out-of-the-money options are cheap because they rarely make money — not because they are bargains.

Being right about direction is not enough. You need to be right about direction, magnitude, and timing simultaneously.

Options have genuine uses — hedging, defined-risk speculation, income generation — but these require experience, specific knowledge, and capital you can genuinely afford to commit.

The beginner who understands all of this is in a far stronger position than the beginner who jumps in because a WhatsApp group showed a screenshot of a 500% options gain. Those screenshots are real. What they do not show is the forty times the premium went to zero before that one winner arrived.

Know what you are using. Know why you are using it. Know what it will cost you when it does not work — because sometimes it will not work. And size every single trade so that when it does not work, it is a lesson rather than a disaster.

That is options, completely and honestly explained.
