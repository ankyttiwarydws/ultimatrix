The Day That Started Fine

Aryan is 27. He has been trading for about eight months — mostly Nifty options, some equity delivery. He is not consistently profitable yet, but he is improving. His account started at ₹1.5 lakh and is currently at ₹1.42 lakh — a small drawdown that he is comfortable with. He has a trading journal. He follows the 2% rule most of the time.

Most of the time.

It is a Tuesday in October. The night before, Aryan had a clear plan. He identified a potential breakout setup on the Nifty daily chart — a flag pattern setting up near 19,800. His plan was to buy a call option if Nifty moved above 19,850 with some strength in the first hour.

The market opens at 9:15 AM. Nifty opens at 19,820 — slightly above yesterday's close. The first fifteen minutes are choppy. At 9:38 AM, Nifty ticks above 19,850 for a moment. Aryan enters: he buys a Nifty 19,900 CE (call option) for ₹65. He buys 75 units — one lot. Total cost: ₹4,875.

His stop is at 19,780 — if Nifty falls back to that level, the breakout has failed.

By 10:05 AM, something shifts. The market-wide sell-off starts. Nothing specific to Nifty — some global concern about US bond yields that Aryan had seen in the morning news but had not weighted heavily enough. Nifty drops from 19,850 back to 19,790, then to 19,765.

His stop-loss level is breached. He exits. His call option has fallen from ₹65 to ₹28.

Loss: 75 × ₹37 = ₹2,775.

By any objective measure, this is a reasonable trade that did not work. He followed his plan. He exited at the stop. He lost 1.96% of his account — just under his 2% rule. This is exactly how risk management is supposed to work.

But Aryan does not feel reasonable. He feels angry.

The Feeling That Starts Everything

Here is what the inside of Aryan's head sounds like at 10:12 AM:

"That was the perfect setup. The flag was clear. The entry was right. The only reason it failed was that stupid US bond yield thing — that's not a trading reason, that's just bad luck. The market will recover. I got shaken out on noise and now it's going to bounce without me and I'll have missed it."

Every part of this thought is wrong. The "stupid US bond yield thing" is not separate from the market — it is the market. Being "shaken out on noise" is what stop-losses do — and sometimes the noise turns into a trend. "The market will recover" is hope, not analysis.

But Aryan does not interrogate any of these thoughts. He feels wronged. His careful plan failed. He wants it back.

At 10:25 AM, Nifty has bounced slightly — from 19,720 back to 19,760. Aryan sees this and interprets it as the recovery he expected. He buys again. Not 75 units this time. 150 units of the same 19,900 CE. The premium is now ₹38.

Cost: 150 × ₹38 = ₹5,700. Double the original position.

No written plan this time. No stop-loss level decided in advance. Just a feeling that the market "owes him" the move he missed.

This is the revenge trade. It has started.

How Revenge Trades Multiply

By 11:00 AM, Nifty has fallen further. The bounce was brief. The 19,900 CE is now trading at ₹18.

Aryan's second position is now showing a loss of 150 × ₹20 = ₹3,000.

He has lost ₹2,775 on the first trade and is down ₹3,000 on the second. Total loss so far today: ₹5,775.

He does not exit. He cannot exit now. Exiting would mean accepting the full loss — it would mean this morning was a complete failure. He cannot let that be true.

By 11:45 AM, Nifty has stabilised. The 19,900 CE is at ₹15. Still losing, but not getting worse.

Then something perverse happens: Nifty drops again. The 19,900 CE falls to ₹9. Aryan's second position is now down ₹4,350.

He buys a third time. 200 units at ₹9. ₹1,800 more.

He is now holding 350 units of the 19,900 CE across three entries at different prices. He is deep in a situation that has no clean exit — and no plan.

At 1:15 PM, Nifty falls to 19,620. The 19,900 CE is at ₹3. His 350 units are worth ₹1,050. He has spent ₹12,375 on this series of trades. His current value is ₹1,050.

He sells. Loss for the day: approximately ₹11,325.

That is 7.98% of his ₹1.42 lakh account — in one morning.

The Week That Followed

You might expect Aryan to stop trading for a few days after this. Most people do, briefly.

He stops for exactly one day.

On Thursday, he trades again. He is not thinking clearly — he is thinking about the ₹11,325 gap in his account. He needs to recover it. He does not think of this as revenge trading again. He thinks of it as "getting back to baseline."

Thursday is a small loss — ₹2,100. He had better discipline, partly because the scar of Tuesday was still fresh.

Friday is a larger loss — ₹4,800. He took a position that was too big, the trade went against him, and he held it too long hoping it would recover. He is noticing a pattern but not naming it correctly. He calls it "bad luck." The correct name is "emotional trading driven by the desire to recover previous losses."

The following week: two more losses totalling ₹6,200.

Three weeks after the original Tuesday, Aryan's account is at ₹1.17 lakh. He has lost ₹25,000 from where he was before that one bad morning.

The original loss on Tuesday was ₹11,325 — painful but survivable. The additional ₹13,675 lost over the subsequent three weeks was entirely the product of the emotional spiral that the first bad day started.

The Anatomy of a Revenge Spiral

Revenge trading does not usually look like one dramatic mistake. It looks like this: a normal bad day that triggers emotional trading, which produces more losses, which deepens the emotional state, which produces worse trading, which produces larger losses.

Each step follows logically from the previous one. And each step feels, from the inside, like a reasonable attempt at recovery.

The key characteristics of revenge trading, as illustrated by Aryan's month:

The original loss was within normal parameters. This is important. Aryan's first loss of ₹2,775 was completely within his risk plan. It was not a disaster. The disaster was manufactured afterward by the emotional response to a normal bad outcome.

Position sizes grew with each new entry. The original trade was 75 units. The first revenge trade was 150. The second was 200. Growing position sizes in a losing session is a signature of revenge trading. When you are thinking clearly, you reduce size after losses. When you are in revenge mode, you increase it.

No stop-loss on any of the revenge trades. The first trade had a stop-loss at 19,780. None of the subsequent entries had pre-defined exits. Revenge trades almost never have planned exits — because the whole point of the trade is emotional, not analytical, and emotional trading is incompatible with the calm pre-trade planning that stop-losses require.

The "recovery target" kept shifting. First Aryan needed to recover the ₹2,775. Then the ₹5,775. Then the full ₹11,325. Then the full ₹25,000. The target kept growing because each new loss reset the baseline, creating a treadmill that could only be escaped by stopping — not by trading more.

The Exit From the Spiral

Aryan's spiral ended not because he made a great trade but because he ran out of emotional energy.

Three weeks into the spiral, on a Friday afternoon, he sat down and went through his trade journal for the month. He forced himself to categorise each trade as either "planned" (trade was written down before entry, stop-loss was set, position size was calculated) or "emotional" (entered reactively, no stop-loss, position size chosen by feeling).

Of the nineteen trades he had placed since that Tuesday, four were planned trades. Fifteen were emotional.

The four planned trades were collectively profitable: +₹3,400. The fifteen emotional trades were collectively a loss: -₹27,400.

This exercise — just sorting trades by whether they had a written plan — showed him in about thirty minutes what three weeks of trading had failed to show him: his strategy was not the problem. His trading was only profitable when he followed it.

He set one new rule: before any trade, he must write the trade setup, entry, stop-loss, and position size in his notebook. If he cannot write it down, he cannot place it. The physical act of writing — which takes ninety seconds — is enough friction to stop most emotional trades before they start.

How to Recognise When You Are Revenge Trading

The tricky thing about revenge trading is that it does not announce itself. Nobody sits down and thinks "I am going to revenge trade now." They think "this setup looks good" or "the market is about to bounce" or "I just need one good trade to recover."

Here are the signs that you are in revenge trading territory:

You took a loss in the last two hours and you are thinking about a new trade. The two hours after a loss are the highest-risk period for emotional trading. Sit out for at least thirty minutes after any loss. If the loss was significant, sit out the rest of the day.

Your new position is larger than your 2% rule allows. If you find yourself calculating a position size and then overriding the number because "this one is different" — stop. The overriding instinct is the problem.

You do not have a written stop-loss for the new trade. If you cannot answer "at what exact price will I exit if this goes wrong" before clicking buy, you are not trading. You are hoping.

The word "recover" is in your mind. The market has no memory of your previous losses. It does not owe you a recovery. The moment your goal shifts from "find a good setup" to "recover what I lost," your trading decisions are no longer connected to market analysis.

You are trading a different instrument than usual. Moving from equity to options, or from index to individual stocks, specifically to make faster money after a loss is a sign that the decision is emotionally driven.

The Practical Rule

After any loss that represents more than 1.5% of your account in a single session, do not place another trade that day.

Write this rule down. Put it somewhere you can see it.

You will violate it once, maybe twice. The losses from those violations will convince you more effectively than anything else that the rule is worth keeping.
