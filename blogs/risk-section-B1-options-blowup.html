This Story Is More Common Than You Think

The person in this story is not unusually reckless. They are not a gambler by nature. They are an ordinary person who got excited about the market, watched a few YouTube videos, opened a trading account, and made a sequence of very normal beginner mistakes that compounded into something devastating.

This story — with different names and slightly different numbers — happens dozens of times every single expiry day in India. Knowing it in detail is the closest thing to protection you have before it happens to you.

Meet Deepak

Deepak is 29. He works in sales at a pharma company in Ahmedabad, earns around ₹55,000 per month, and has been saving reasonably well for three years. By mid-2023, he has ₹3.8 lakh in a savings account doing nothing at 3.5% interest, and he is frustrated about it.

He finds trading YouTube channels. He watches a lot of them. Bank Nifty options trading looks incredible — people showing ₹50,000 profits from a single trade in twenty minutes, ₹1 lakh in an afternoon. He watches for two months before doing anything. He feels prepared.

He opens a Zerodha account. He puts ₹3 lakh in — keeping ₹80,000 aside as an emergency fund. He tells himself he will only risk a small amount at first.

The first two weeks he just watches, not trading. The third week he takes his first trade — a Bank Nifty call option — and makes ₹4,200 in one session. He is shaking with excitement when he checks the screen. He calls his college friend and tells him about it.

The fourth week he makes ₹11,000 across three trades. In one month, his ₹3 lakh has become ₹3.17 lakh.

He feels like he understands this now.

The Thursday That Changed Everything

It is a Thursday. Bank Nifty weekly expiry day.

Deepak has been watching Bank Nifty all morning. It has been in a tight range since open — moving between 44,200 and 44,500, going nowhere. He is sitting at his office desk, phone on the side, half attending a team call and half watching the chart.

At 12:40 PM, Bank Nifty breaks below 44,200. It looks like a breakdown. Deepak has seen this pattern in videos — "bearish breakdown from consolidation." He buys Bank Nifty Put options. The strike is 44,000 PE. The premium is ₹85 per unit. He buys 10 lots.

One lot of Bank Nifty options is 15 units. 10 lots = 150 units. Total premium paid: 150 × ₹85 = ₹12,750.

This is actually fine so far. ₹12,750 is a reasonable amount to risk on a single trade. He has ₹3 lakh in his account. This is about 4% of his capital — slightly above the 2% rule but not catastrophically so.

The problem has not happened yet.

Bank Nifty continues to fall. By 1:15 PM it is at 44,050. Deepak's puts are showing a profit of about ₹7,000. He is excited.

Then something happens that no chart pattern predicted. A news headline hits — something about a positive outcome in an RBI-related announcement. Within eight minutes, Bank Nifty reverses violently. It does not drift upward. It launches. 44,200. 44,400. 44,650.

Deepak's 44,000 PE options collapse. The premium falls from ₹85 to ₹38 as Bank Nifty rockets through his strike. He is watching his profit of ₹7,000 evaporate, turn negative, and become a loss of ₹7,050 all within twelve minutes.

He should exit here. He knows this on some level. But ₹7,050 loss feels too painful to book. And Bank Nifty is moving so fast he is not thinking clearly.

This is the moment where the story goes wrong.

The Decision That Cost Everything

Instead of exiting, Deepak decides to average down — to buy more puts at the new, cheaper price and lower his average cost. Bank Nifty is at 44,650 now. The 44,000 PE is trading at ₹22. He buys 20 more lots.

20 more lots = 300 more units. 300 × ₹22 = ₹6,600 more capital deployed.

His reasoning: "Bank Nifty has moved 450 points up in twelve minutes. It cannot keep going. It will come back down. If it drops even 200 points I will recover everything."

This reasoning is not crazy in isolation. Market moves do reverse. But Deepak has made four separate mistakes in one decision:

Mistake 1 — Averaging down on options, not equity. Averaging down on equity shares — buying more of a stock that fell — is already a questionable strategy. Averaging down on options on expiry day is a different category of dangerous. Options are burning time value every hour, especially on expiry day. Even if Bank Nifty came back down 200 points, the premium might not recover proportionally — time decay would have eaten into the value while he waited.

Mistake 2 — Throwing good money after bad. The original trade thesis was a breakdown below 44,200. Bank Nifty is now at 44,650 — 450 points above where the thesis was formed. The trade is not just losing. It is categorically wrong. Adding more money to a categorically wrong trade is not strategy. It is hope.

Mistake 3 — Losing track of total exposure. Deepak is now holding 10 original lots plus 20 new lots = 30 lots total. 30 lots = 450 units. At ₹22 premium, his total current position value is ₹9,900. But his total capital at risk has grown. He has spent ₹12,750 + ₹6,600 = ₹19,350 on this single position.

Mistake 4 — No stop-loss on either entry. There was no pre-defined exit on either the original trade or the new purchase. Deepak is managing this entire position by feel, in real time, while sitting in a work meeting, on expiry day.

2:30 PM

Bank Nifty pushes higher still. 44,850. 45,000.

The 44,000 PE options are now almost worthless. Trading at ₹6 per unit. Deepak's 450 units are worth ₹2,700. He has lost ₹16,650 on this position.

He stares at the screen. He cannot exit now — booking a ₹16,650 loss feels unbearable. He tells himself: "There is one hour left. Bank Nifty might pull back into close. These puts could recover to ₹20 or ₹30."

This thought — the hope that the last hour will save him — is the most dangerous thought in trading.

He makes one more decision. He checks his account balance: ₹2,83,000 remaining. He buys 60 more lots of the 44,000 PE at ₹6.

60 lots = 900 units. 900 × ₹6 = ₹5,400.

Total spent on this position now: ₹12,750 + ₹6,600 + ₹5,400 = ₹24,750. Total units: 450 + 900 = 1,350 units.

His logic: "If Bank Nifty falls even 100 points in the last hour, the premium might go from ₹6 to ₹30. 1,350 units × ₹24 gain = ₹32,400. I could recover everything and more."

This logic is mathematically possible. It is also the last coherent thought Deepak has before the clock runs out.

3:30 PM

Bank Nifty closes at 45,120.

The 44,000 PE expires worthless. All 1,350 units expire at ₹0.

Deepak's ₹24,750 is gone. Completely. Not reduced. Gone.

He sits at his desk for a long time. His remaining account balance is ₹2,75,250 — he lost ₹24,750 today. That is painful but not catastrophic.

Except the story is not over.

Because Deepak does not stop.

The Next Three Weeks

Deepak spends Thursday night telling himself this was a one-off bad day. He had the right idea — Bank Nifty did eventually have a significant fall the following week. He was just early. He just averaged too aggressively on expiry day.

He trades again the following week. And the week after. By the time five weeks have passed, his ₹3 lakh has become ₹1.1 lakh.

He does not tell anyone. The friend he called after his early profits — he avoids that conversation now. He does not tell his family. He checks his account in private, feeling a mixture of shame, desperation, and the persistent conviction that he is "close to figuring it out."

The ₹1.9 lakh he has lost in five weeks is gone from the market. He will not get it back from trading. He will have to earn it back the regular way — months of salary, careful saving, starting over.

At some point — three days after his account hits ₹1.1 lakh — Deepak stops trading. Not because he made a rational decision to stop. Because he cannot bring himself to watch his account fall further.

He sits on ₹1.1 lakh in his trading account for two months, doing nothing with it, not sure what to do next.

What Actually Went Wrong — And When

Reading this story, it is tempting to point to the 2:30 PM decision — buying 60 lots of nearly worthless options — as the moment everything broke. But the real breaking points were earlier and quieter.

The first break: no stop-loss on the original entry. If Deepak had decided before entering: "I will exit this position if Bank Nifty rises above 44,400 — because at that point the breakdown thesis is invalidated" — the maximum loss on the original trade was about ₹9,000. Painful, but recoverable. The trade would have been over at 1:30 PM.

The second break: averaging down on expiry day options. Averaging down on an options position that is moving against you on expiry day is one of the highest-risk decisions in all of retail trading. Time decay accelerates through the afternoon on expiry day. An option that seems cheap at ₹22 can be worthless in two hours regardless of what the underlying does. The "it will come back" logic that works sometimes in equity does not translate to expiring options.

The third break: total exposure grew without awareness. Deepak never paused to calculate his total risk as it grew. The original ₹12,750, the additional ₹6,600, the final ₹5,400 — he experienced these as three separate smaller decisions rather than one cumulative ₹24,750 all-in bet on Bank Nifty reversing in ninety minutes. Thinking in individual trade amounts rather than total exposure is a cognitive trap that growing position sizes rely on.

The fourth break: not stopping after the bad day. The ₹24,750 loss on Thursday was recoverable. The subsequent five weeks of trading while in an emotional state — trying to recover, lacking the discipline to sit out, continuing to use expiry day options as the primary instrument — turned a painful but survivable loss into something that changed his financial situation meaningfully.

What Deepak Did Differently After

Six months later, Deepak is still trading — but differently.

He has a strict rule: no new positions in the last two hours of expiry day. If he is not already in a trade by 1:30 PM on expiry Thursday, he does not enter one.

He uses the position sizing formula for every trade. His maximum loss per trade is 2% of whatever his current account holds. He does not deviate.

He never averages down on options. Ever. If the trade goes against him, he exits at his planned stop. Full stop.

His win rate is not dramatically better than it was. He still loses on roughly four out of every ten trades. But his account is slowly, quietly growing — because his losses are small and his wins are allowed to be full-sized. The math, finally, is working in his direction.

He has not had another ₹24,750 day. Not because the market has been kind to him. Because he decided — specifically, with rules written down — that it would not happen again.
