The Message That Started It

It is a Wednesday evening in February. Suresh gets a WhatsApp message from his colleague Rakesh.

Rakesh is not a professional trader. He is a project manager at the same IT firm where Suresh works, and he has been trading for about two years. In the office, Rakesh sometimes mentions his trading profits in conversation — he made ₹15,000 in one week on a Nifty trade, he bought a small-cap stock that doubled in four months. Suresh has noticed these mentions. Rakesh seems to know what he is doing.

The message reads:

"Bhai, insider source in a trusted group gave a tip on XYZ Pharma. CEO of a foreign company is supposed to make an acquisition announcement next week. Stock is at ₹87 right now. Could easily 2-3x on the news. I'm putting in ₹2 lakhs. You should look at it."

Suresh is 38. He has been saving carefully for seven years — a fixed monthly amount from his salary, discipline he is genuinely proud of. He has ₹4.8 lakh in a savings account. It was meant to eventually become a down payment on a flat.

He searches for XYZ Pharma. It is a small company he has never heard of. Daily trading volume is low. There is almost no recent news about it. The chart shows it has been quietly drifting between ₹80 and ₹95 for several months.

He asks Rakesh: "How reliable is this group?"

Rakesh says the group has been right three times in the last two months. One stock doubled, one went up 40%, one went up 25%.

Suresh sits with this for two days. On Friday evening, with the stock still at ₹89, he transfers ₹2 lakh from his savings account into his trading account. He buys 2,247 shares of XYZ Pharma at an average of ₹88.95.

He does not set a stop-loss. He does not know where to set one on a stock he knows nothing about. He decides to "hold it for the news."

The First Week: Waiting

The first week, nothing happens. XYZ Pharma trades between ₹85 and ₹92. No acquisition announcement. No news.

Suresh checks the stock about fifteen times a day. He is in the Telegram group now — Rakesh added him. There are 12,000 members. The group is active. People are posting about XYZ Pharma constantly: "strong accumulation happening," "promoter bought 50,000 shares this week," "deal announcement imminent."

None of these claims have sources. But they feel like information.

The second week, the stock drifts down to ₹81. Suresh is down about ₹18,000 on paper. The Telegram group remains bullish. Someone posts a screenshot of a financial news article — but when Suresh reads the full article, it is about a different pharmaceutical company entirely. This detail is not acknowledged in the group. The post gets 200 thumbs-up reactions.

Suresh holds. The "news" is still coming, according to the group.

The Stock Starts to Move — In the Wrong Direction

Week three. XYZ Pharma falls below ₹75.

In the Telegram group, the tone shifts slightly. Some members ask what is happening. The admins post messages about "shakeout before the big move" and "weak hands being flushed out." A few members post that they have averaged down, buying more at ₹75 because "the thesis is intact."

Suresh does not average down — not because he is disciplined, but because he does not have more money easily available.

He is now down roughly ₹32,000 on paper. The flat down payment that ₹2 lakh was meant to eventually contribute to feels further away.

Then he searches more carefully for information on XYZ Pharma. What he finds is not reassuring. The company's most recent quarterly results showed declining revenues. The promoter holding has been decreasing — promoters have been selling shares for three consecutive quarters. There is no analyst coverage from any reputable firm. The company's order book is thin.

There is no acquisition rumour anywhere in any legitimate financial news source. Not a hint of it.

He goes back to Rakesh and asks about the "insider source."

Rakesh pauses. Then admits that he does not actually know who runs the Telegram group. He found it through another group. He does not know the admins personally. He trusted it because of the previous three tips that went well.

What Was Actually Happening

Here is what Suresh did not know when he bought — and what, had he known, would have changed his decision immediately.

The XYZ Pharma tip was a pump-and-dump scheme. This is a pattern that has existed for as long as there have been financial markets.

It works like this: someone — usually a group of operators — buys a large position in a low-volume, thinly-traded small-cap stock at a low price, quietly, without moving the price significantly. This is possible in illiquid stocks because small orders can accumulate without attracting attention.

Then they create a distribution network — Telegram groups, WhatsApp chains, social media accounts — and release a "tip" about the stock. The tip always sounds credible: acquisition news, large order win, government contract, foreign partnership. The exact details do not matter much. The goal is to create buying pressure from retail investors who hear the tip and rush in.

As retail investors buy, the price rises. The operators sell into this rising price — the demand created by their own tip provides the exit liquidity they needed.

By the time the stock starts falling and the retail buyers begin to wonder why the "news" never materialised, the operators are long gone. The 12,000-member Telegram group is full of retail holders who are all stuck, all hoping, and all eventually selling at a loss to each other.

The three previous "successful" tips from this group were almost certainly the same mechanism on smaller stocks — planned pumps where early retail buyers who sold quickly showed profits, creating the social proof that attracted the next wave of buyers including Rakesh, including Suresh.

The Exit

By week five, XYZ Pharma is at ₹58.

The Telegram group has gone quiet on XYZ. New tips are being floated for different stocks. Some members are angry, posting about being "scammed." The admins are posting less frequently.

Suresh's ₹2 lakh is now worth approximately ₹1.30 lakh. He is down ₹70,000.

He sells. He cannot explain why he waited this long except that each week he held, the loss became more real and more difficult to accept. The selling feels like surrendering to reality that he had been refusing to look at directly.

After brokerage and the paperwork of moving money back to his savings account, he has recovered ₹1.27 lakh from his original ₹2 lakh. Loss: ₹73,000.

He does not tell his wife for six weeks. When he does, the conversation is very difficult. The ₹73,000 was not abstract trading capital. It was earmarked money — part of a larger goal for their family. It will take him almost three years to save it back.

The Mechanics of Why This Worked on Suresh

Suresh is not naive. He is a software professional with a postgraduate degree. He reads the news. He is generally careful with money. Understanding how this happened to him is more useful than simply cataloguing the mistakes.

Tip 1: Social proof from a trusted peer. The tip did not come from a stranger on the internet. It came from a colleague Suresh had worked with for three years, someone who had demonstrated knowledge of trading through office conversations. This is how these schemes propagate most effectively — through real social networks where the original trust is genuine even if the information is not.

Rakesh was also a victim. He lost money on XYZ Pharma too. His previous "successful" tips from the group had been real enough profits to earn Suresh's trust — but those were likely early pumps where Rakesh happened to buy and sell at the right time by chance.

Tip 2: Confirmation bias in the group. Once Suresh was in the Telegram group, every piece of information he encountered reinforced the original decision to buy. This is not accidental — it is the design of these groups. Admins post positive-sounding content constantly. Members who post doubts are ignored or told they are "weak hands." The information environment is engineered to support holding, not questioning.

Tip 3: The sunk cost trap. Once Suresh had invested ₹2 lakh, each week of holding produced a deeper attachment to the position. At ₹81 per share, selling would have crystallised a ₹18,000 loss. At ₹75, the loss was ₹32,000. By ₹68, exiting felt almost impossible. The increasing loss made the exit feel increasingly unacceptable — which is the exact opposite of how rational decision-making works. The bigger a loss becomes, the more important it is to stop it, not the more important it becomes to wait for a recovery.

Tip 4: No plan, no stop-loss, no exit criteria. Had Suresh set a simple rule before buying — "if this stock falls 15% from my entry, I exit regardless of the news" — his maximum loss would have been approximately ₹30,000. He would have exited at around ₹75, taking a painful but manageable ₹30,000 loss rather than a relationship-straining ₹73,000 one.

The tip was the trigger. The absence of a stop-loss was what turned a bad trade into a disaster.

How to Protect Yourself From This — Specifically

Recognising a pump-and-dump setup is not as difficult as it seems once you know what to look for.

Check the stock's average daily volume. If a stock normally trades 20,000 shares per day and a tip suggests it is about to move significantly, ask yourself: if 12,000 Telegram group members each buy 1,000 shares, that is 1.2 crore shares of buying pressure on a stock that normally trades 20,000 per day. This artificial demand is what creates the initial price rise — and it is also what creates the trap, because when the buying stops, there is no natural demand base to hold the price.

Low-volume stocks are the preferred vehicle for pump-and-dump schemes because they move easily and the scheme requires less capital to execute.

Search for the "news" in legitimate sources before acting on it. The XYZ Pharma acquisition rumour existed only in the Telegram group. It was not in Economic Times, Moneycontrol, Business Standard, NSE filings, or any BSE announcement. A real acquisition rumour — even a leaked, informal one — leaves traces in legitimate media. A fabricated one does not. Thirty minutes of searching would have told Suresh everything he needed to know.

Look at promoter shareholding trends. On the NSE or BSE website, every listed company's shareholding pattern is publicly available by quarter. A stock being tipped as a "massive buying opportunity" whose own promoters have been selling their shares for three consecutive quarters is sending a clear signal. Promoters know their own company better than any Telegram group admin.

Apply the 2% rule regardless of conviction. Even if Suresh had believed the tip was legitimate — even if he was certain the acquisition would be announced — the correct position size was 2% of his trading capital, not 42% of his life savings. The conviction felt real. The tip felt credible. These feelings are not risk management.

If the tip is genuinely good, 2% of your capital will still give you a meaningful profit when it plays out. If the tip is wrong or fraudulent, 2% is survivable. Putting ₹2 lakh of a ₹4.8 lakh savings account into a single illiquid stock on the basis of a WhatsApp message is not a trading decision. It is a bet.

On Rakesh

Suresh and Rakesh did not speak about XYZ Pharma for several months after it was over. When they eventually did, it was a surprisingly honest conversation.

Rakesh had lost ₹65,000. He felt guilty about having passed the tip to Suresh. He had genuinely believed it was good information. He had not known, at the time, that the three previous "successful" tips from the group were also likely orchestrated — he had simply experienced them as profitable trades and drawn the wrong conclusion.

Neither of them blamed the other, in the end. They were both retail investors who trusted a social network they had not properly evaluated, in a financial instrument they did not fully understand, on a company they had not researched.

The Telegram group still exists. Under a slightly different name, it has 18,000 members now.
